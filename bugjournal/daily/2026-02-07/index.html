### 📖 阅读部分（45秒）

**The Bystander Effect**

The bystander effect refers to the phenomenon in which individuals are less likely to offer help in an emergency situation when other people are present. Research has shown that as the number of bystanders increases, the likelihood that any one person will intervene decreases. This occurs for two main reasons: **diffusion of responsibility** — the belief that someone else will take action — and **pluralistic ignorance** — the tendency to look to others for cues and, seeing no one act, assume that the situation is not actually an emergency. Understanding the bystander effect has important implications for public safety and emergency response training.

The professor describes a classic psychology experiment conducted in a laboratory setting.

In the experiment, participants were placed either alone in a room or in a group of three. While completing a questionnaire, smoke began to slowly fill the room through a vent — simulating an emergency.

When participants were **alone**, approximately **75%** reported the smoke within two minutes.

However, when participants were in a **group of three**, only about **38%** reported the smoke — and many waited much longer before acting. Researchers noticed that participants in the group would glance at each other, and since no one was reacting with alarm, they seemed to conclude that the situation was not serious.
