Research Trajectory#

Ismail Bayram is a pragmatic Turkish researcher in agricultural/veterinary science, with a career spanning 1999–2025. His core expertise lies in the nutritional science of poultry (quail, broilers, laying hens) and ruminants (sheep, buffalo, dairy cows). His academic trajectory began with basic feed additives (probiotics, vitamins, fishmeal substitutes), and between 2006 and 2013 he developed a distinctive research focus on natural herbal plant extracts (oregano, aniseed, poppy seeds, mint, etc.). In 2008, his work on ultrasound-based in vivo carcass composition prediction became his most-cited single publication (42 citations). After 2018, his research shifted toward transition period metabolic management in dairy cows and the immunomodulatory functions of plant-derived extracts.

Overall, this researcher represents a steady contributor to regional applied agricultural science rather than a global leader — with an h-index of 11 and 479 total citations, but only 31 in the past five years, indicating that his academic impact was concentrated primarily in 2003–2013 and has declined significantly in recent years. His publication venues are primarily Turkish and Eastern European regional specialty journals (with Research in Veterinary Science and Meat Science being among his highest-tier outlets), and he has no top conference papers.

⚠️ Important data quality note: The paper list contains obvious same-name conflation — papers on AISI 4042 steel weld microstructure (2010), ethyl acetate reactive distillation simulation (2013, 2022), and brine purification (2018) are completely inconsistent with this researcher’s animal science background, and are very likely mis-attributed database entries from a different person sharing the same name. These are excluded from the academic analysis.

Breakthrough Works#

1. The use of ultrasound to predict the carcass composition of live Akkaraman lambs (2008)#

Description: Real-time ultrasound scanning was used to predict post-slaughter carcass composition (muscle thickness, fat thickness, loin eye area, etc.) in live Akkaraman sheep, eliminating the traditional reliance on post-mortem carcass data and providing a non-invasive tool for breeding selection and commercial quality prediction.

Why It Wasn’t Done Before: Early ultrasound equipment was bulky and prohibitively expensive. Portable real-time ultrasound devices only became broadly accessible in animal science in the early 2000s, and their adoption required the accumulation of breed-specific calibration equations. Prior to this work, breed-specific correction data for Turkish native breeds (Akkaraman) was lacking, limiting practical deployment of the technology.

Impact: Became this researcher’s most-cited paper (42 citations), advancing the use of ultrasound technology in sheep breeding and production management across the Near East and Central Asia, and providing a methodological reference for in vivo carcass quality assessment.

2. Effects of probiotics: alone and in a mixture of Biosacc plus Zinc Bacitracin on the caecal microflora of Japanese quail (2003)#

Description: Systematically compared the effects of single probiotics versus a probiotic + zinc bacitracin combination on cecal microflora in quail, exploring the feasibility of using probiotics to replace antibiotic growth promoters.

Why It Wasn’t Done Before: Regulatory pressure from the EU’s progressive ban on antibiotic growth promoters (fully enacted in 2006) was beginning to emerge in the early 2000s, creating urgent demand for scientific evidence on alternatives. Quantitative data on probiotic effects at the level of poultry cecal microbiota was extremely limited at the time, and microbial detection techniques (traditional culture methods) were only beginning to be systematically applied to avian nutrition research.

Impact: 35 citations. Provided early experimental evidence for the herbal-probiotic combination approach as an antibiotic alternative, and holds citation value in the antibiotic replacement literature for this field.

3. Stress responses to comparative handling procedures in sheep (2013)#

Description: Systematically compared the effects of various routine husbandry procedures — milking, shearing, weighing, and others — on stress physiological parameters such as cortisol and blood biochemistry in sheep, quantitatively assessing the relative welfare impact of different handling operations.

Why It Wasn’t Done Before: A science-based framework for animal welfare assessment only became systematized in Europe in the mid-to-late 2000s, and standardized measurement methods and reference values for welfare indicators (such as cortisol assays) only became operationally feasible at the small-to-medium farm level during this period. Prior research had largely consisted of isolated measurements on single procedures, lacking systematic comparative designs across handling types.

Impact: 29 citations. Provides an empirical basis for cross-procedure comparison in sheep welfare assessment, with reference value for both animal welfare science and farm management practice.

4. Effects of aniseed (Pimpinella anisum L.) on egg production, quality, cholesterol levels, hatching results and the antibody values in blood of laying quails (2007)#

Description: Comprehensively evaluated the effects of aniseed as a natural feed additive on quail laying performance, egg quality, serum cholesterol, and immune antibody levels, integrating the multidimensional functions of plant essential oils/extracts into a single research framework.

Why It Wasn’t Done Before: EU regulations progressively banning antibiotic growth promoters from 2003 onward drove systematic research demand for natural alternatives. Meanwhile, identification and quantitative analysis of bioactive components in essential oils (e.g., anethole) only matured in the early 2000s, making it possible to establish active component–effect relationships for plant-based additives.

Impact: 24 citations. Became one of the important early references for the application of herbal plants in poultry production, establishing a methodological template for the subsequent wave of similar studies (oregano, mint, pomegranate, etc.).

5. Effects of short chain fatty acid (SCFA) supplementation on performance and egg characteristics of old breeder hens (2007)#

Description: Investigated the effects of short-chain fatty acids (propionic acid, butyric acid, etc.) on laying performance and egg quality in aged breeder hens, proposing an actionable nutritional intervention strategy for late-cycle breeder flocks.

Why It Wasn’t Done Before: The mechanisms by which short-chain fatty acids support gut health and mucosal integrity were only beginning to be clarified in monogastric animal nutrition in the early 2000s. Prior SCFA research had focused primarily on piglets; application to aged poultry breeder flocks was an unexplored niche. Stability issues with commercial SCFA products (coated SCFAs) were also resolved around this time.

Impact: 21 citations. Provided a non-antibiotic gut health maintenance strategy for the management of aged breeder flocks, with practical reference value in the breeder production literature.

Research Directions#

  • Natural herbal plants and plant extracts as feed additives for poultry/ruminants (oregano, aniseed, poppy seeds, mint, pomegranate, myrtle, etc.)
  • Optimization of poultry production performance (laying rate, egg quality, and growth performance in laying hens, broilers, and quail)
  • Ruminant nutrition and health management (sheep carcass prediction, dairy cow transition period metabolism, buffalo lactation)
  • Probiotics and gut microbiota modulation as alternatives to antibiotic growth promoters
  • Animal welfare and stress physiology assessment
  • Near-infrared spectroscopy (NIR/FT-NIR) for rapid feed composition analysis

Methodological Evolution#

The research methodology evolved through three distinct phases. In the early period (1999–2006), the work relied primarily on conventional feeding trials with measurements limited to basic production performance (body weight gain, feed conversion ratio) and standard blood biochemical parameters, with relatively simple experimental designs and small sample sizes. In the middle period (2007–2013), the methodology became considerably richer: ultrasound in vivo imaging was introduced for non-destructive carcass composition prediction; antioxidant enzyme activities (SOD, CAT, MDA) and lipid peroxidation markers were added; factorial designs with multiple treatment combinations were established; and egg yolk fatty acid profiling was incorporated into the evaluation framework, expanding the research scope from production performance alone to food quality and safety. In the later period (2015–present), near-infrared spectroscopy (NIR/FT-NIR) was adopted to build quantitative calibration models for rapid feed quality testing; more complex metabolomic indicators were introduced in dairy cow studies (ketosis biomarkers, acute-phase proteins, etc.); and in vitro digestibility systems (DAISY Incubator) were applied for multi-species digestibility comparisons. Overall, the methodological evolution reflects a pattern of following and adopting techniques as they became accessible in the industry, rather than original development of frontier methods.

Field Impact#

Ismail Bayram’s contributions to animal nutrition represent an accumulation of regional applied science rather than paradigm-shifting impact. His core value lies in building a substantial body of localized data on natural feed additive applications for breeds common to Turkey and surrounding regions (Akkaraman sheep, Japanese quail, Anatolian buffalo), filling gaps in the international literature where these local livestock breeds receive insufficient attention. Against the backdrop of the EU ban on antibiotic growth promoters, he has consistently provided empirical evidence for herbal-based alternatives. However, his overall impact is limited: only 31 citations in the past five years indicates significantly diminished academic influence; his publication venues lack high-impact-factor journals; and the generalizability of his findings is constrained by specific breeds and geographic regions. His work serves primarily the practical needs of Turkey’s livestock industry rather than advancing the theoretical frontier of animal nutrition science.
