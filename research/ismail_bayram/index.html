Research Trajectory#

Ismail Bayram is a practical researcher in the field of agricultural/ veterinary science in Turkey. His research career spanned from 1999 to 2025, with his main focus being on the nutritional sciences of poultry (quails, broilers, laying hens) and ruminants (sheep, buffalo, dairy cows). His academic journey began with basic feed additives such as probiotics, vitamins, and fish meal alternatives. Between 2006 and 2013, he developed a research direction focused on natural herbal extracts like oregano, fennel, poppy seeds, and mint. In 2008, his paper using ultrasound technology to predict carcass composition was the most cited paper (42 citations). After 2018, his research shifted towards metabolic management during the dairy cow transition period and the immunomodulatory effects of plant extracts.

In terms of overall academic influence, this researcher is a stable contributor to regional practical agricultural science rather than a global leader. With an h-index of 11 and 479 total citations, however, the number of citations in the last 5 years was only 31, indicating that his academic influence was mainly concentrated from 2003 to 2013, with significant decline in recent years. His publications are primarily in Turkish and Eastern European professional journals, such as Research in Veterinary Science and Meat Science.

Breakthrough Works#

1. Use of Ultrasound to Predict Carcass Composition of Live Akkaraman Lambs (2008)#

Description: Using real-time ultrasound scanning technology, this study predicts the carcass composition of live Akkaraman sheep after slaughtering, including muscle thickness, fat thickness, and eye muscle area. This method avoids the limitations of traditional slaughter methods, providing a non-invasive tool for breeding selection and product quality prediction.

Why couldn’t it be done before: Ultrasound equipment was bulky and expensive in the early days. Portable real-time ultrasound instruments became popular in animal science only in the early 2000s, and it required a large amount of data on breed-specific correction equations. There was no available data on breed-specific corrections for Turkish local breeds like Akkaraman, which limited the implementation of this technology.

Impact: This paper is one of the most cited papers by this researcher (42 citations). It has promoted the use of ultrasound technology in sheep breeding and production management in the Middle East and Central Asia. It provides a methodological reference for evaluating the quality of live animals.

2. Effects of Probiotics: Alone and in a Mixture of Biosacc plus Zinc Bacitracin on the Caecal Microflora of Japanese Quails (2003)#

Description: This study systematically compared the effects of probiotics alone and probiotics combined with zinc bacitracin on the intestinal microflora of quails. It explored the possibility of using probiotics as a substitute for antibiotic growth promoters.

Why couldn’t it be done before: The EU’s ban on antibiotics in feedstuffs began in the early 2000s, which forced the industry to seek scientific evidence for alternative solutions. There was little quantitative research on probiotics’ effects on quail intestinal microflora, and microbial detection techniques (traditional culture methods) were only beginning to be used in poultry nutrition research.

Impact: This paper has been cited 35 times. It provided early experimental evidence for the use of herbal additives as a substitute for antibiotics. It is valuable in the literature on antibiotic alternatives.

3. Stress Responses to Comparative Handling Procedures in Sheep (2013)#

Description: This study systematically compared the effects of various routine management operations such as milking, shearing, and weighing on stress physiological parameters like cortisol levels and blood biochemical indicators in sheep. It quantified the relative impact of different operations on animal welfare.

Why couldn’t it be done before: The scientific evaluation framework for animal welfare was only gradually established in Europe during the mid-2000s. Standardized methods for measuring welfare indicators, such as cortisol measurements, became operational at the small and medium-sized farm level during this period. Previous research was often isolated measurements of individual operations, lacking systematic comparisons across different types of operations.

Impact: This paper has been cited 29 times. It provides an empirical basis for comparing multiple operations in sheep welfare evaluation. It is valuable in animal welfare science and ranch management practices.

4. Effects of Aniseed (Pimpinella anisum L.) on Egg Production, Quality, Cholesterol Levels, Hatching Results, and Antibody Values in Blood of Laying Quails (2007)#

Description: This study systematically evaluated the effects of fennel as a natural feed additive on quail egg production, egg quality, serum cholesterol levels, hatching results, and antibody values in blood. It integrated the multi-dimensional functions of plant essential oils/extracts into a single research framework.

Why couldn’t it be done before: The EU’s ban on antibiotics in feedstuffs began in 2003, which led to a demand for systematic research on alternative natural additives. At the same time, the identification and quantification of essential oil active components (such as fennel oil) became more advanced in the early 2000s. This made it possible to study the effectiveness of plant additives.

Impact: This paper has been cited 24 times. It is one of the important early papers on the use of herbal plants in poultry production. It provided a methodological template for many subsequent studies on similar topics, such as oregano, mint, and pomegranate.

5. Effects of Short Chain Fatty Acid (SCFA) Supplementation on Performance and Egg Characteristics of Old Breeder Hens (2007)#

Description: This study explored the effects of short chain fatty acids (such as propionic acid and butyric acid) on the egg production performance and egg quality of old breeding hens. It proposed operational nutritional interventions for groups of breeding hens in the final stage of their lives.

Why couldn’t it be done before: The role of short chain fatty acids in intestinal health and mucosal integrity was only gradually understood in the field of monogastric animal nutrition in the early 2000s. Previous research on SCFA mainly focused on piglets, and its application in older poultry breeding hens was still unknown. The stability issues of commercial products containing SCFA were also addressed during this period.

Impact: This paper has been cited 21 times. It provided a non-antibiotic solution for maintaining intestinal health in old breeding hens. It is valuable in the literature on breeding hen management.

Research Areas#

  • Nutrition and health management of ruminants (prediction of dairy cow carcass composition, metabolic management during dairy cow transition period, milk production of buffalo)
  • Probiotics and intestinal microbial regulation as a substitute for antibiotics
  • Animal welfare and stress physiological assessment
  • Application of near-infrared spectroscopy in rapid analysis of feed components

Methodological Evolution#

The research methodology has evolved through three clear stages. In the early stage (1999-2006), traditional feeding experiments were used, with measurement indicators limited to basic production performance (body weight gain, feed conversion rate) and conventional blood biochemical parameters. The experimental design was relatively simple, and the sample size was small. In the middle stage (2007-2013), the methodology became more comprehensive: ultrasound imaging technology was introduced for non-destructive prediction of carcass composition, antioxidant enzyme activity (SOD, CAT, MDA) and lipid peroxidation indicators were added, a factorial design for multi-factor composite treatments was established, and蛋黄脂肪酸谱分析 was included in the evaluation system. The research dimension expanded from single production performance to food quality and safety. In the late stage (2015-present), near-infrared spectroscopy was used to establish quantitative calibration models for rapid feed quality testing. Metabolomics indicators such as ketosis biomarkers and acute-phase proteins were introduced into dairy cow studies, and the DAISY Incubator was also used to compare digestive characteristics of multiple species. Overall, the evolution of methodology is a follow-up adoption of industry technology rather than original development of cutting-edge methods.

Domain Impact#

Ismail Bayram’s contributions to animal nutrition are part of regional practical scientific accumulation rather than paradigm-shifting impacts. His core value lies in establishing a wealth of localized data on natural feed additives for common breeds in Turkey and surrounding areas, filling the gap in international literature that neglected these local livestock breeds. Under the EU policy banning antibiotics in feedstuffs, he continues to provide empirical evidence for herbal alternatives. However, his overall influence is limited: only 31 citations in the last 5 years indicate a significant decline in academic influence. His publications lack high-impact factor journals, and the scalability of his research results is limited by specific breeds and geographical regions. His research serves the production needs of Turkish animal husbandry rather than pushing the theoretical frontiers of animal nutrition.
