Research Trajectory#

Yiran Chen’s academic trajectory shows three clear transitions. The first phase (2003-2007) focused on optimizing the power consumption of traditional computer architectures, with Deterministic Clock Gating (DCG) being a representative example. This was a solid foundation in classical VLSI design. The second phase (2008-2015) marked his transition into the emerging storage field: as technologies like Spin-Transfer Torque RAM (STT-RAM), Phase Change Memory (PCM), and Memristor moved from laboratory research to engineering, Chen quickly took charge of NVM device modeling, reliability analysis, cache architecture integration, and neuromorphic computing. He published extensively in top conferences such as HPCA, DAC, and ICCD, laying the foundation for this field’s methodology. The third phase (2016-present) saw a breakthrough transformation: his 2016 paper on Structured Sparsity Learning received 2,478 citations, marking his entry into deep learning compression with a hardware-aware perspective. Since then, he has continued to produce influential work in distributed training communication compression, ReRAM-based accelerators (PipeLayer/GraphR), and AI security (anti-attacks, federated learning defense). Chen’s ability to bridge the gap between hardware and AI lies in his focus on “how to run AI efficiently on limited hardware.” He integrates device physics, circuit design, architecture, and algorithm optimization into a unified research framework. This rare all-around perspective allowed him to lead the way in hardware-algorithm co-design when this became mainstream.

Breakthrough Works#

1. A novel architecture of the 3D stacked MRAM L2 cache for CMPs (2009)#

Description: The first systematic design of three-dimensional stacked MRAM as an L2 cache for multi-core processors. It evaluated the entire process from circuit modeling, thermal analysis to performance/power trade-offs at the architectural level, proving the engineering feasibility of NVM replacing traditional SRAM caches.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Key conditions were not mature by 2009: first, there was no proven compact circuit model for STT-RAM magnetic tunnel junctions; second, three-dimensional chip stacking technology was just in the academic prototype stage; third, previous NVM research mainly focused on device physics, lacking system-level methodologies to integrate them into existing microprocessor storage hierarchies. Chen’s team had both magnetic device modeling and computer architecture skills, filling this interdisciplinary gap.

Impact: It received 453 citations, pioneering the research paradigm of using NVM as a cache alternative. This led to numerous follow-up studies on STT-RAM/PCM/RRAM cache architectures in the following decade, establishing a methodological framework for evaluating devices-circuit-architecture interactions.

2. Memristor Crossbar-Based Neuromorphic Computing System: A Case Study (2014)#

Description: A systematic implementation and analysis of neuromorphic computing systems based on memristor cross-arrays. This work covered complete engineering issues such as analog matrix-vector multiplication, non-linear activation implementations, and device variation tolerance training. It was a landmark work in the development of memristor-based neuromorphic computing from concept to engineering system.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Memristors were first fabricated and verified by HP Labs in 2008. It took several years for sufficient experimental data on device characteristics (switching ratio, variation distribution, durability) to be available for modeling. More importantly, mapping memristor analog conductance to neural network weights required a combined background in circuit design and neural network training theory—teams with this cross-disciplinary perspective were rare before 2014.

Impact: It received 338 citations, becoming a foundational case study in memristor-based neuromorphic computing. Many subsequent “Computing-in-Memory” accelerator works built upon this work.

3. Learning Structured Sparsity in Deep Neural Networks (2016)#

Description: Proposed a structured sparsity learning method that automatically learns structured sparse patterns for hardware acceleration during training by applying group Lasso regularization to filter, channel, convolution kernel shapes, and network layer depths. This allowed DNNs to learn structures directly applicable to hardware acceleration without specialized sparse computing hardware.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Previous network compression methods (such as Han Song’s 2015 unstructured pruning) produced random sparsity that could not be efficiently utilized by general-purpose hardware. Specialized sparse computing engines were required. The key insight was the lack of realization that the granularity of sparsity constraints could be elevated from weight level to structure level, with group regularization naturally emerging during training. Chen’s team shifted hardware feasibility requirements back into algorithm design, making this hardware-aware ML methodology highly advanced at the time.

Impact: It received 2478 citations, making it one of his most cited papers. It changed the research paradigm in DNN compression, leading to structured pruning becoming the mainstream technology for model deployment in industry, and inspiring many subsequent channel pruning and layer pruning methods.

4. TernGrad: Ternary Gradients to Reduce Communication in Distributed Deep Learning (2017)#

Description: Proposed using ternary (-1, 0, +1) gradients instead of full-precision floating-point gradients for gradient synchronization during distributed training. Theoretical convergence was proven, and experiments showed significant reduction in communication bandwidth requirements while maintaining almost no loss of accuracy.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Large-scale distributed deep learning training had not become common before 2017. Communication bottlenecks became prominent with the rise of large models like AlexNet/ResNet. Previous quantitative research focused on weight compression for inference, and using gradient quantization for training compression required a deep understanding of SGD convergence dynamics. The insight that the direction of gradients rather than exact values was more important emerged in 2017 when the need for distributed training became urgent.

Impact: It received 1053 citations, pioneering the subfield of gradient compression for distributed learning and communication optimization. It directly led to developments like 1-bit SGD and SignSGD, having a lasting impact on large-scale AI training infrastructure.

5. PipeLayer: A Pipelined ReRAM-Based Accelerator for Deep Learning (2017)#

Description: Proposed a pipelined ReRAM-based accelerator for deep learning inference. By mapping neural network layers to ReRAM cross-arrays and designing cross-layer pipeline data streams, it achieved a combination of in-memory computing and high throughput inference, outperforming GPU solutions in terms of energy efficiency and speed.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Three conditions needed to be met: (1) The ReRAM device model had to be mature enough to support system-level simulation; (2) The computational mode of deep learning inference (mainly dense matrix multiplication) had to be well analyzed, making its compatibility with analog cross-arrays obvious; (3) ADC/DAC design technology and peripheral circuit capabilities had to be sufficient to support mixed-signal system design. Before 2017, there was insufficient experimental data on ReRAM chip prototypes, and the modeling of DNN tolerance for accuracy loss was not mature.

Impact: It received 750 citations, becoming a landmark work in ReRAM-based in-memory computing accelerators. It established the principle of “eliminating ReRAM write latency bottlenecks with pipelines,” leading to research on CIM accelerators like ReTransformer and Lattice.

Research Areas#

  • Non-volatile memory architecture (STT-RAM/RRAM/PCM) device modeling and system integration
  • In-memory computing based on memristors/ReRAMs and neuromorphic accelerators
  • Deep neural network compression and hardware-aware efficient inference (structured pruning, low-rank decomposition, quantization)
  • AI security and robustness (anti-attack generation and defense, data poisoning, federated learning security)
  • Distributed machine learning and federated learning (communication compression, client selection, heterogeneity handling)
  • Neural architecture search (NAS) and AutoML

Methodological Evolution#

Chen’s methodological evolution shows a clear “spiral rise from bottom to top, then from top to bottom.” In the early stages (2003-2010), SPICE simulation and VLSI analysis tools were central. The methodology was rooted in circuit design, focusing on precise modeling at the device and circuit levels. From 2010 to 2015, the methodology expanded to the architecture level—introducing statistical variation analysis, Monte Carlo probability modeling, and cross-layer (device-circuit-architecture) collaborative evaluation frameworks. This “from physics to system” vertical integration became its core competitiveness. Since 2016, the methodology has changed direction: while retaining hardware implementation feasibility constraints, it shifted towards algorithm optimization. SSL and TernGrad exemplify the reverse thinking of “hardware constraints driving algorithm design.” After 2019, the methodology expanded into system security, using game theory (attack-defense confrontation) and privacy computing frameworks to analyze federated learning security. In recent years, it has extended to LLM inference optimization. The overall trajectory is: single-layer precise modeling → cross-layer collaborative optimization → hardware-algorithm joint design → system security analysis. Each expansion was based on the deep accumulation of the previous stage.

Domain Impact#

Yiran Chen’s impact on the field is reflected in three dimensions: First, in the emerging memory field, his work on STT-RAM/RRAM (2008-2015) established the foundational methodology for NVM computer architecture research, turning “using NVM to replace/supplement SRAM” from physical concept into an engineering-oriented research agenda. Second, in deep learning systems, his papers on SSL (2478 citations) and TernGrad (1053 citations) are textbook works. The former defined the technical direction of structured pruning, while the latter pioneered the subfield of gradient compression. Together, they have received over 3500 citations, profoundly influencing the technology roadmap for AI chip deployment and large-scale training infrastructure. Third, in AI security and trustworthy AI, his team has made foundational contributions to anti-attack samples, poisoning attacks, and federated learning defense, promoting a systematic understanding of AI system vulnerabilities in academia. His unique value lies in being one of the few scholars who have published highly cited works in both top computer architecture conferences (HPCA/MICRO/ISCA) and machine learning conferences (NeurIPS/ICML/CVPR/ICLR). He continues to act as a “translator” and “bridge builder” between the hardware and AI communities. His h-index of 65 and total citations of nearly 18,000 are top-tier in this composite field.
