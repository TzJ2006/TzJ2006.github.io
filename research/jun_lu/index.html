Research Trajectory#

Jun Lu’s academic trajectory shows a clear two-phase evolution. The first phase (1989–2013) focused on numerical calculations of electromagnetic fields, particularly the Harmonic Balance Finite-Element Method (HB-FEM) and the design of high-frequency planar transformers. This period laid a solid theoretical foundation in the analysis of nonlinear magnetic fields. A key turning point occurred around 2013–2015: with the rise of the global electric vehicle industry and smart grid policies, his research on high-frequency magnetic devices and wireless charging became highly valuable from an industrial perspective. As a result, his research shifted towards EV wireless charging, microgrid energy management, and optimization of energy storage systems. The second phase (2014–2023) reached its peak in 2018 with a comprehensive review paper titled “366 Citations”. This work established Jun Lu as a leading scholar in the field of EV wireless charging. It is worth noting that papers published after 2022, such as those on superconducting materials, Riemannian geometry, quantum networks, and insect-computer hybrid speakers, show severe author data contamination. These works should be considered as contributions by different researchers rather than part of Jun Lu’s academic trajectory.

Breakthrough Works#

1. Analysis of the DC Bias Phenomenon by the Harmonic Balance Finite-Element Method (2011)#

Description: Applied harmonic balance finite element method to analyze the DC bias phenomenon in transformers, establishing a complete mathematical model that can accurately calculate harmonic distributions in nonlinear saturated magnetic fields.

Why it couldn’t be done before: The magnetic saturation caused by DC bias is a highly nonlinear problem. Traditional time-domain finite element methods require excessive computational resources and are difficult to converge. The HB-FEM theory was not yet mature at that time, lacking fixed-point iteration algorithms for handling non-linear hysteresis curves. Moreover, computer performance was insufficient to support three-dimensional harmonic solutions.

Impact: Provided an authoritative simulation tool for the design and protection of DC bias in power system transformers. Published in IEEE Transactions on Power Delivery, with 51 citations, this work became a benchmark document in this field.

2. Characterizations of High Frequency Planar Transformer With a Novel Comb-Shaped Shield (2011)#

Description: Proposed a comb-shaped shielding structure for high-frequency planar transformers, systematically characterizing their electromagnetic shielding efficiency and eddy current loss characteristics. This provided a new structural solution for on-chip power supplies and wireless charging couplers.

Why it couldn’t be done before: The skin effect and proximity effect were complex in high frequencies, making traditional analytical models ineffective. Fine three-dimensional finite element simulations combined with experimental verification were needed, but such simulation tools and micro-manufacturing processes were not widely available in the early 2000s.

Impact: With 45 citations, this work promoted the evolution of planar transformers towards higher frequencies and power densities. It laid the foundation for research on integrated magnetic devices in subsequent LLC resonant converters.

3. Autoregressive with Exogenous Variables and Neural Network Short-Term Load Forecast Models for Residential Low Voltage Distribution Networks (2014)#

Description: Developed a hybrid ARIMAX and neural network model for short-term load forecasting in residential low-voltage distribution networks. This model identified key exogenous variables that affect demand, enabling accurate predictions of total electricity consumption and peak demand the next day.

Why it couldn’t be done before: The behavior of end-users in low-voltage distribution networks was highly random. Traditional statistical models had insufficient generalization capabilities. Deep learning hadn’t become widely used at that time, and early neural networks faced overfitting and training difficulties. With the widespread adoption of smart meters, high-resolution electricity data became available for the first time, making data-driven modeling possible.

Impact: With 80 citations, this work became an important benchmark in the field of distribution network demand forecasting. It directly inspired research on prediction modules in microgrid energy management systems.

4. Review of static and dynamic wireless electric vehicle charging system (2018)#

Description: Conducted a systematic review of static and dynamic wireless electric vehicle charging technologies, covering induction coupling principles, coil design, power electronics topologies, interoperability standards, and safety aspects. A technology roadmap was proposed.

Why it couldn’t be done before: This review was possible because there was a wealth of original research on wireless charging technology from 2012 to 2018, which led to its industrialization. The development of Qi standards and the SAE J2954 draft made standardization discussions possible. Jun Lu’s ten years of experience in planar transformers, induction couplers, and EV charging systems allowed him to conduct this authoritative review.

Impact: With 366 citations, this work was the most influential among all papers in his research list. It established Jun Lu as a leading scholar in the field of EV wireless charging, with many subsequent studies citing it as a reference.

5. Short-term load forecasting for microgrid energy management system using hybrid HHO-FNN model with best-basis stationary wavelet packet transform (2020)#

Description: Applied optimal basis stationary wavelet packet transformation to decompose load signals, and combined with Harris-Ho optimization algorithm for parameter tuning of feedforward neural networks. This created a dedicated short-term load forecasting framework for microgrid energy management.

Why it couldn’t be done before: The Harris-Ho algorithm was introduced in 2019, making it a relatively new meta-heuristic optimization tool at that time. Hybrid prediction frameworks combining deep learning with intelligent optimization algorithms required sufficient computational resources and standardized microgrid operation datasets. Neither of these existed before 2019.

Impact: With 105 citations, this work was Jun Lu’s most influential original research in the field of load forecasting and energy management. It established a research paradigm for “signal decomposition + meta-heuristic optimization + neural network” in the microgrid domain.

Methodological Evolution#

Jun Lu’s methodology has undergone three paradigm shifts. The first phase (1989–2012) focused on mathematical analysis: the harmonic balance method transformed nonlinear differential equations into complex algebraic systems, which were then discretized using finite element methods to form the unique computational electromagnetics toolchain of HB-FEM. The evolution of this method progressed from simple harmonic truncation to fixed-point iteration and neural network-assisted hysteresis modeling. The second phase (2013–2018) shifted towards system-level modeling and control: the research scope expanded from magnetic devices to power electronics topologies and microgrid hierarchical control architectures. The methodology evolved from numerical simulations to experimental platform verification and hardware-in-the-loop testing. MEMS radio frequency switches and wireless charging couplers were also designed with a focus on micro-manufacturing processes. The third phase (2019–2023) fully introduced a data-driven approach: combining wavelet packet decomposition, meta-heuristic optimization (HHO, GWO, SSA), and deep neural networks (LSTM). This reflected the trend of shifting research in energy systems towards machine learning methods. Jun Lu effectively applied his signal processing experience to time-series forecasting problems, maintaining methodological consistency.

Domain Impact#

Jun Lu’s contributions to the field are evident at two levels: At the foundational method level, his HB-FEM work (1989–2017) provided a mature computational tool for analyzing DC bias in power transformers and high-frequency nonlinear magnetic fields. His findings were published in top journals such as IEEE Transactions on Magnetics and IEEE Transactions on Power Delivery, making his work valuable for academic reference. At the applied level, his 2018 review paper on EV wireless charging (366 citations) became a essential reference for newcomers to this field, effectively driving research agenda setting. Overall, Jun Lu is a leading scholar focused on engineering applications: with an h-index of 27 and approximately 2852 total citations, he has established a solid academic influence in the intersection of power electronics and smart grids. However, his papers published in top journals are only 13, and his citations in the last five years (186) are relatively limited, indicating that his peak period has passed. His current influence primarily relies on long-tail citations accumulated over time.
