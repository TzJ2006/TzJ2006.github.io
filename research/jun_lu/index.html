Research Trajectory#

Jun Lu’s academic career follows a clear two-phase arc. The first phase (1989–2013) was devoted to numerical electromagnetic field computation, with a particular focus on the Harmonic Balance Finite-Element Method (HB-FEM) and high-frequency planar transformer design, building a robust methodological foundation in nonlinear magnetic field analysis. A pivotal shift occurred around 2013–2015: the global surge in the electric vehicle industry and smart grid policy initiatives gave his accumulated expertise in high-frequency magnetics and wireless charging high industrial relevance, steering his research toward EV wireless charging, microgrid energy management, and energy storage system optimization. The second phase (2014–2023) peaked with a highly cited survey published in 2018 (366 citations), establishing him as a leading voice in the EV wireless charging field. Notably, papers appearing after 2022—covering topics such as superconducting materials, Riemannian geometry, quantum networks, and insect-computer hybrid speakers—indicate severe name-collision data contamination and should be attributed to different researchers, excluded from any assessment of his scholarly output.

Landmark Contributions#

1. Analysis of the DC Bias Phenomenon by the Harmonic Balance Finite-Element Method (2011)#

Description: Systematically applied the Harmonic Balance Finite-Element Method (HB-FEM) to the analysis of the DC bias phenomenon in transformers, establishing a complete mathematical model capable of accurately computing harmonic distributions in nonlinearly saturated magnetic fields.

Why it wasn’t done before: DC bias-induced magnetic saturation is a strongly nonlinear problem; traditional time-domain finite-element methods were computationally prohibitive and prone to convergence failures. Early HB-FEM theory was still immature, lacking the fixed-point iterative algorithms needed to handle hysteresis loop nonlinearity, and available computing power was insufficient to support fully coupled three-dimensional harmonic solutions.

Impact: Provided an authoritative simulation tool for DC bias protection and design in power system transformers. Published in IEEE Transactions on Power Delivery with 51 citations, it became a benchmark reference in this sub-field.

2. Characterizations of High Frequency Planar Transformer With a Novel Comb-Shaped Shield (2011)#

Description: Proposed a comb-shaped shielding structure for high-frequency planar transformers, systematically characterizing its electromagnetic shielding effectiveness and eddy-current loss behavior, and offering a novel structural approach for on-chip power supply and wireless charging coupler design.

Why it wasn’t done before: At high frequencies, skin effect and proximity effect interact in complex ways that invalidate traditional analytical models. Accurate three-dimensional finite-element simulation combined with experimental validation was required, yet simulation tools of this precision and the microfabrication processes needed were not widely accessible in the early 2000s.

Impact: Cited 45 times, it advanced planar transformers toward higher frequencies and greater power density and established a design paradigm for integrated magnetics in subsequent LLC resonant converter research.

3. Autoregressive with Exogenous Variables and Neural Network Short-Term Load Forecast Models for Residential Low Voltage Distribution Networks (2014)#

Description: Developed a hybrid ARIMAX and neural network model for short-term load forecasting in residential low-voltage distribution networks, identifying key exogenous variables driving demand and enabling accurate next-day total consumption and peak demand prediction.

Why it wasn’t done before: End-user behavior at the low-voltage distribution level is highly stochastic, and traditional statistical models lacked sufficient generalization ability. Deep learning had not yet matured, and early neural networks suffered from overfitting and training difficulties. It was the widespread rollout of smart meters at this time that made high-resolution historical consumption data available for the first time, making data-driven modeling feasible.

Impact: Cited 80 times, it became an important benchmark in distribution network demand forecasting and directly inspired subsequent research on forecasting modules within microgrid energy management systems.

4. Review of static and dynamic wireless electric vehicle charging system (2018)#

Description: Provided a comprehensive review of static and dynamic wireless EV charging technologies, covering inductive coupling principles, coil design, power electronics topologies, interoperability standards, and safety considerations, while proposing a technology roadmap.

Why it wasn’t done before: The publication of this review depended on the large body of original research accumulated between 2012 and 2018 as wireless charging technology transitioned from laboratory to industry. Maturation of the Qi standard and the drafting of SAE J2954 made standardization discussions possible, while the author’s decade of prior work on planar transformers, inductive couplers, and EV charging systems gave him the standing to synthesize the field authoritatively.

Impact: Cited 366 times—the most influential work in his entire publication list—this paper established Jun Lu as a representative scholar in EV wireless charging and has been widely cited as a survey entry point by subsequent researchers.

5. Short-term load forecasting for microgrid energy management system using hybrid HHO-FNN model with best-basis stationary wavelet packet transform (2020)#

Description: Applied best-basis stationary wavelet packet transform for load signal decomposition, combined with a feedforward neural network tuned by the Harris Hawks Optimization (HHO) algorithm, to construct a dedicated short-term load forecasting framework for microgrid energy management.

Why it wasn’t done before: The HHO algorithm was only proposed in 2019 and was a relatively new metaheuristic tool at the time. Hybrid forecasting frameworks combining deep learning with intelligent optimization algorithms required sufficient computational resources and standardized microgrid operational datasets, both of which were inadequate before 2019.

Impact: Cited 105 times, this is the author’s most influential original work at the intersection of forecasting and energy management, establishing the “signal decomposition + metaheuristic optimization + neural network” three-stage forecasting paradigm as a research template in the microgrid domain.

Methodological Evolution#

Jun Lu’s methodology has undergone three paradigm shifts. The first phase (1989–2012) was centered on mathematical analysis: the harmonic balance method transformed nonlinear differential equations into systems of complex algebraic equations, and combined with finite-element discretization, it formed the distinctive HB-FEM computational electromagnetics toolchain. The hallmark of methodological progress in this phase was the upgrade from simple harmonic truncation to fixed-point iteration and neural-network-assisted hysteresis modeling. The second phase (2013–2018) shifted toward system-level modeling and control: the research scope expanded from magnetic devices to power electronics converter topologies and microgrid hierarchical control architectures, and the methodology extended from numerical simulation (FEM) to experimental platform validation and hardware-in-the-loop testing, with MEMS RF switches and wireless charging coupler design also incorporating microfabrication perspectives. The third phase (2019–2023) fully embraced the data-driven paradigm: the combination of wavelet packet decomposition, metaheuristic optimization (HHO, GWO, SSA), and deep neural networks (LSTM) reflects the broader migration of energy systems research toward machine learning methods. The author adeptly transferred his signal processing expertise to time-series forecasting problems, maintaining methodological coherence throughout.

Field Impact#

Jun Lu’s core contributions to the field operate on two levels. At the foundational methods level, his HB-FEM series (1989–2017) provided mature computational tools for analyzing DC bias in power transformers and high-frequency nonlinear magnetic fields; the resulting work, published in top journals including IEEE Transactions on Magnetics and IEEE Transactions on Power Delivery, retains enduring reference value in the academic community. At the applied level, his 2018 EV wireless charging review (366 citations), by virtue of its comprehensiveness and timeliness, became essential reading for researchers entering the field, effectively shaping the research agenda. Overall, Jun Lu represents a capable, application-oriented engineering scholar: with an h-index of 27 and approximately 2,852 total citations, he holds a solid academic influence at the intersection of power electronics and smart grids. However, with only 13 top-venue papers and relatively modest recent citations (186 over the last five years), his peak period has passed and his current influence rests primarily on the long-tail citations of his historical output.
