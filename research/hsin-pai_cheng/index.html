Research Trajectory#

Hsin-Pai Cheng is a researcher specializing in efficient AI systems and edge deployment. His academic trajectory can be divided into three clear stages. From 2016 to 2018, he focused on the IBM TrueNorth neuromorphic computing platform, studying the quantization of neural networks under hardware constraints and their robustness against adversarial attacks. This stage laid the foundation for his dual perspective on algorithms and hardware.

From 2019 to 2020 was a period of high productivity and influence. He led the review article “Low-Power Computer Vision” (63 citations), which defined the subfield of low-power computer vision. In the same year, several NAS papers (MSNet, AutoShrink, SwiftNet) focused on architecture search for IoT/edge devices. The paper “Ordering Chaos” (MLSys, 58 citations) solved the engineering bottleneck in memory scheduling for irregular NAS networks, marking a key step forward in practical deployment of NAS research.

After 2021, his output slowed significantly (almost no publications in 2021). From 2022 to 2024, his work focused on zero-sample NAS and efficient Transformers. In 2025-2026, new papers emerged in the field of end-to-end autonomous driving systems (RoCA, FALO, generative scene generation). Given the lack of institutional information, it is speculated that he has entered the industrial sector, shifting his focus to edge deployment of autonomous driving systems. Overall, he is a “efficiency engineer” rather than a pioneer in a new field, but he has made significant contributions to low-power AI deployment.

Breakthrough Works#

1. Low-Power Computer Vision: Status, Challenges, and Opportunities (2019)#

Description: This paper systematically reviewed the current state, challenges, and opportunities in low-power computer vision, covering model compression, hardware acceleration, and mobile device deployment. It became a landmark review article in this field.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Before 2018, the mobile AI ecosystem was not yet mature. Lightweight models like MobileNet/ShuffleNet were just starting to emerge, NAS methods were not widely used, and there was insufficient data for actual chip applications (such as Kirin, A-series NPUs), making it impossible to produce a systematic review based on real-world data. 2019 was the right time for industrial implementation and academic breakthroughs.

Impact: With 63 citations, this paper became his most cited work. It provided a unified framework and reference for low-power vision research, promoting systematic attention to “edge computing efficiency.”

2. Ordering Chaos: Memory-Aware Scheduling of Irregularly Wired Neural Networks for Edge Devices (2020)#

Description: This paper proposed a memory-aware scheduling algorithm for irregularly wired neural networks, enabling efficient operation on edge devices with limited memory. It filled the gap between NAS design and practical deployment.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Irregularly wired NAS networks like Random Wiring Network only emerged in 2018-2019, so the problem of memory scheduling was new. Previously, regular networks like VGG and ResNet had fixed computation graphs, making scheduling simple. Meanwhile, MLSys, a system-and-machine learning conference, was founded in 2019, providing a platform for publishing papers. The key insight was to treat irregular graphs found in NAS research as scheduling optimization problems rather than pure algorithm problems.

Impact: Published at MLSys 2020 (a top system conference), it received 58 citations. It clarified the paradigm that “NAS should not only search for accuracy but also deployability,” influencing subsequent research on hardware-aware NAS.

3. MAT: A Multi-strength Adversarial Training Method to Mitigate Adversarial Attacks (2018)#

Description: This paper proposed a multi-intensity adversarial training strategy, which significantly improved the robustness of DNNs against various types of adversarial attacks while reducing the loss of accuracy for clean samples.

Why it couldn’t be done before: The problem of adversarial examples was first proposed by Goodfellow et al. in 2014, but early defense methods (such as single-intensity training) quickly became ineffective against strong attacks. The emergence of PGD attacks and C&W attacks in 2017-2018 revealed the limitations of single-intensity training, leading to the need for multi-intensity strategies. In terms of computational resources, generating multiple rounds of adversarial examples and training was too costly before 2017.

Impact: With 29 citations, this paper was one of his most cited algorithmic works. It made significant contributions to methods for improving adversarial robustness and provided a foundation for subsequent research on neuromorphic computing security (AdverQuil, 2019).

4. NASGEM: Neural Architecture Search via Graph Embedding Method (2020)#

Description: This paper introduced graph embedding methods into NAS, using graph neural networks to encode the topological structure of architectures and constructing performance predictors. This resulted in a more flexible and scalable NAS framework based on estimators.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Predictor-based NAS was just starting to emerge before 2019, and the encoding methods were mostly simple sequences or manual features. Graph neural networks began to mature in 2018-2019 (such as GraphSAGE, GIN), but using GNNs for architectural topology encoding became feasible only in 2019-2020.

Impact: Published at AAAI 2020, it received 24 citations. It promoted the NAS technology of “graph-based architecture representation” and was one of his two peak outputs in 2020, along with Ordering Chaos.

5. Towards Decentralized Deep Learning with Differential Privacy (2019)#

Description: This paper proposed algorithms for protecting privacy in decentralized learning frameworks, maintaining good convergence speed and communication efficiency while respecting privacy budgets. It was aimed at federated learning scenarios.

Why it couldn’t be done before: The combination of differential privacy and deep learning (such as DP-SGD) was proposed by Abadi et al. in 2016, but it mainly targeted centralized scenarios at that time. Decentralized training without parameter servers had high communication costs and complex privacy analysis before 2018. With advancements in distributed optimization theory and communication compression technology, conditions were met to handle both dimensions simultaneously in 2019.

Impact: With 36 citations, this paper received significant attention in the field of privacy-protected distributed learning, demonstrating its research scope from hardware efficiency to system security.

Methodological Evolution#

Cheng’s methodological evolution shows a three-stage transformation from “single-point optimization” to “system-level coordination” and then to “task-driven”. In the early stages (2016-2018), empirical algorithms were used: compensation learning strategies were proposed for IBM TrueNorth’s quantization losses, and multi-intensity training data augmentation was designed for adversarial attacks. These methods were direct and verifiable, but their focus was limited to a single dimension.

From 2019 to 2020, his methodology evolved into a systematic approach of “searching + graph structure + constraint awareness”. NAS works generally introduced graph representations (NASGEM used GNNs to encode architectural topologies, MSNet/SwiftNet used graph propagation for meta-knowledge). The optimization goals expanded from single accuracy to multi-objectives such as accuracy × latency × memory. Ordering Chaos further transformed the problem into scheduling optimization, crossing the boundaries between algorithms and systems. After 2022, zero-sample NAS (ZiCo-BC) emerged, attempting to replace expensive architecture evaluations with training-independent metrics, representing a extreme example of efficiency pursuit. Papers on autonomous driving from 2025 onwards reflect an engineering transformation from “how to design efficient models” to “how to deploy in real-world closed-loop systems”.

Domain Impact#

Cheng has a clear but medium-scale influence in the field of low-power/edge AI: h-index 13, total citations 450. Two of his core papers (Low-Power CV review with 63 citations, Ordering Chaos with 58 citations) contributed about one-fourth of the citations. His contributions mainly lie in “breaking through the last mile between NAS and practical deployment” - this engineering problem is often overlooked in academia. The publication of Ordering Chaos at MLSys marked the formal recognition of this issue by the system community. Only 7 citations in the past five years indicate that his academic activity has significantly declined, suggesting that he has shifted his focus to industrial research. Overall, he is a valuable contributor to the field of edge AI deployment, but he has not reached the level of being a pioneer in a new research paradigm.
