Research Trajectory#

Hsin-Pai Cheng is a researcher focused on efficient AI systems and edge deployment. His academic career falls into three clear phases. From 2016 to 2018, he used the IBM TrueNorth neuromorphic platform as an entry point to investigate neural network quantization and adversarial robustness under hardware constraints, developing a systematic understanding of how to deploy AI under compute limitations. This phase established his dual perspective spanning both algorithms and hardware.

The period from 2019 to 2020 was his most prolific and influential. His co-authored survey Low-Power Computer Vision (63 citations) systematically defined the low-power computer vision subfield; multiple NAS papers published the same year (MSNet, AutoShrink, SwiftNet) focused on architecture search for IoT and edge devices; and Ordering Chaos (MLSys 2020, 58 citations) addressed the engineering bottleneck of memory scheduling for irregular NAS-generated networks — a critical step toward real-world deployment.

Output slowed notably after 2021 (with almost no publications that year), and the 2022–2024 period centered on zero-shot NAS and efficient Transformers. New papers appearing in 2025–2026 on end-to-end autonomous driving (RoCA, FALO, generative scene unrolling) — combined with the absence of institutional affiliation — suggest he has moved into industry, with his focus shifting toward edge deployment for autonomous driving systems. Overall, he is an “efficiency engineer” type of researcher rather than a paradigm-founding figure, but he has made solid contributions in the niche area of low-power AI deployment.

Breakthrough Works#

1. Low-Power Computer Vision: Status, Challenges, and Opportunities (2019)#

Description: A systematic survey of the state of research, challenges, and opportunities in low-power computer vision, covering model compression, hardware acceleration, mobile deployment, and more. It became a landmark reference for the field.

Why it couldn’t have been done earlier: Before 2018, the mobile AI ecosystem was not yet mature — lightweight models like MobileNet and ShuffleNet were just emerging, NAS had not yet become widespread, and real-world chip deployments (Kirin, Apple NPUs) lacked the scale needed to support a data-backed systematic survey. 2019 was the pivotal window when industrial deployment was accelerating and academic methods were flourishing.

Impact: With 63 citations, it became his most-cited paper, providing a unified problem framework and benchmark reference for subsequent low-power vision research, and driving the academic community toward a more systematic focus on edge compute efficiency.

2. Ordering Chaos: Memory-Aware Scheduling of Irregularly Wired Neural Networks for Edge Devices (2020)#

Description: For the irregular computation graphs produced by NAS and randomly wired networks, this paper proposes a memory-aware scheduling algorithm that enables such networks to run efficiently on memory-constrained edge devices, bridging the engineering gap between NAS design and practical deployment.

Why it couldn’t have been done earlier: Irregularly wired NAS networks (e.g., Random Wiring Networks) didn’t appear until 2018–2019, making their memory scheduling a genuinely new problem. Prior regular networks (VGG, ResNet) had fixed computation graphs with trivial scheduling. MLSys — the systems-plus-ML cross-disciplinary venue — also only launched in 2019, providing a publication home. The key insight was framing the irregular NAS graph as a scheduling optimization problem rather than a purely algorithmic one.

Impact: Published at MLSys 2020 (a top systems venue), with 58 citations. It established the idea that “NAS should optimize for deployability, not just accuracy,” influencing the direction of subsequent hardware-aware NAS research.

3. MAT: A Multi-strength Adversarial Training Method to Mitigate Adversarial Attacks (2018)#

Description: Proposes a multi-strength adversarial training strategy that mixes adversarial examples of varying perturbation magnitudes during training, significantly improving DNN robustness against diverse adversarial attacks while reducing accuracy degradation on clean samples.

Why it couldn’t have been done earlier: The adversarial example problem was introduced by Goodfellow et al. in 2014, but early defenses (e.g., single-strength adversarial training) were quickly found to fail against strong attacks. The emergence of PGD and C&W attacks in 2017–2018 exposed the limitations of single-strength training and created demand for multi-strength strategies. On the compute side, generating multi-round adversarial examples during training was prohibitively expensive before 2017.

Impact: 29 citations, his most-cited early algorithmic work, with a clear technical contribution to adversarial robustness training — and it laid the groundwork for subsequent neuromorphic computing security research (AdverQuil, 2019).

4. NASGEM: Neural Architecture Search via Graph Embedding Method (2020)#

Description: Introduces graph embedding into NAS by using graph neural networks to encode the topology of neural architectures, building a performance predictor and enabling a more flexible, scalable predictor-based NAS framework.

Why it couldn’t have been done earlier: Predictor-based NAS was just getting off the ground before 2019, with architecture encodings typically relying on simple sequences or handcrafted features. Graph neural networks matured between 2018 and 2019 (GraphSAGE, GIN, etc.), and applying GNNs to encode architectural topology was a synthesis that only became feasible around 2019–2020.

Impact: Published at AAAI 2020, 24 citations, advancing the “graph-based architecture representation” line of NAS research. Together with Ordering Chaos, it forms the dual peak of his 2020 output.

5. Towards Decentralized Deep Learning with Differential Privacy (2019)#

Description: Proposes an algorithm for achieving differential privacy in decentralized distributed learning, maintaining good convergence speed and communication efficiency while respecting privacy budgets — targeting federated learning scenarios.

Why it couldn’t have been done earlier: Combining differential privacy with deep learning (e.g., DP-SGD) was proposed by Abadi et al. in 2016, but the focus was on centralized settings. Decentralized training (without a parameter server) was too communication-heavy before 2018, and the privacy analysis was complex. Advances in distributed optimization theory and communication compression techniques created the conditions by 2019 to handle both dimensions simultaneously.

Impact: 36 citations, with strong attention at the intersection of privacy-preserving and distributed learning, reflecting the breadth of his research vision extending from hardware efficiency to system security.

Methodological Evolution#

Cheng’s methodological evolution follows a three-stage arc: from “point optimization” to “system-level co-design” to “task-driven engineering.” The early period (2016–2018) was characterized by empirical algorithms — proposing compensation learning to address quantization loss on IBM TrueNorth, and designing multi-strength data augmentation against adversarial attacks. The methods were direct and verifiable, but scoped to individual dimensions.

From 2019 to 2020, his methodology upgraded to a systematic paradigm of “search + graph structure + constraint awareness”: NAS work broadly adopted graph representations (NASGEM using GNNs to encode architecture topology, MSNet/SwiftNet using graph propagation as meta-knowledge), and optimization targets expanded from single-objective accuracy to multi-objective trade-offs across accuracy, latency, and memory. Ordering Chaos went further, modeling the problem as scheduling optimization and crossing the boundary between algorithms and systems. After 2022, zero-shot NAS (ZiCo-BC) emerged — attempting to replace expensive architecture evaluation with training-free proxy metrics, representing the extreme of efficiency pursuit. The autonomous driving papers from 2025 onward reflect an engineering transition from “how to design efficient models” to “how to deploy them in real closed-loop systems.”

Field Impact#

Cheng has a clear but moderately scaled influence in the low-power/edge AI domain: h-index 13, 450 total citations, with two core papers (the Low-Power CV survey at 63 citations, Ordering Chaos at 58) together accounting for roughly a quarter of his total citation count. His contribution is best characterized as “closing the last mile between NAS and real deployment” — an engineering problem often overlooked in academia, but one whose publication at MLSys signals formal recognition by the systems community. With only 7 citations in the past five years, his academic activity has dropped substantially, suggesting his center of gravity has shifted to industry R&D. Overall, he is a valuable contributor in the niche of edge AI deployment, though he has not reached the paradigm-founding status of a field-defining figure.
