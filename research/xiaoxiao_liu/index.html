Research Trajectory#

It should be noted that there is an obvious name confusion issue in this paper list. The papers cover completely unrelated fields such as cancer biology, GPU architecture, plant immunity, hyperspectral image processing, genomics, and LLM prompt engineering. They obviously come from multiple researchers with the same name. Based on publication years, citation numbers, and thematic consistency, at least three different Xiaoxiao Liu can be identified: (1) Molecular Oncology方向 (contributing to most of the 451 citations, including 62 citations in the highest-cited miR-1271 pancreatic cancer paper and papers of the level of Nature Communications/JEM/PNAS); (2) Computer Architecture/Image Processing direction (GPU memory design, hyperspectral denoising); (3) LLM/AI security direction (prompt engineering, security evaluation, etc. from 2023 - 2025). Breakthrough Works#

1. miR-1271 inhibits migration, invasion, and epithelial-mesenchymal transition by targeting ZEB1 and TWIST1 in pancreatic cancer cells (2016)#

Description: For the first time, it was systematically demonstrated that miR-1271 inhibits the migration, invasion, and epithelial-mesenchymal transition of pancreatic cancer cells by targeting both ZEB1 and TWIST1, two core EMT transcription factors. This provides a dual-target miRNA regulation framework for the metastasis mechanism of pancreatic cancer.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Before 2016, the cost of high-throughput sequencing for miRNAs was still high, and systematic studies on miRNA regulatory networks in pancreatic cancer were limited. The mechanism by which ZEB1 and TWIST1 were co-targeted by the same miRNA had not been revealed. It was only with the popularization of NGS technology and the maturity of bioinformatics analysis tools that this dual-target regulation axis could be systematically screened and verified.

Impact: This paper became the most cited paper of this researcher, with 62 citations. It promoted the establishment of a multi-target miRNA regulation strategy in pancreatic cancer metastasis research and has important reference value for subsequent studies on EMI-related therapeutic targets.

2. Antitumor immunity enhances the therapeutic effects of p53 activation on acute myeloid leukemia (2019)#

Description: Published in Nature Communications, it demonstrated that the orally active p53-MDM2 interaction inhibitor DS-5272 not only exerts anti-tumor effects by directly activating the p53 signal in AML cells, but also synergistically enhances the host’s anti-tumor immune response. This revealed the synergistic mechanism between targeted therapy and tumor immunity.

Why it couldn’t be done before: p53-MDM2 inhibitors were long considered to act through cell-autonomous pathways. The long-term separation between immunology research and targeted drug research made it difficult to discover this synergy relationship. Around 2019, the integration of immun oncology and small molecule targeted therapy became a research hotspot. The maturity of single-cell immunotyping technologies such as CyTOF (published with supporting datasets in the same year) made it possible to accurately quantify the dynamic changes of immune cell subsets.

Impact: Published in Nature Communications with 42 citations, this paper expanded the p53-targeted therapy research for AML from a single-cell killing paradigm to an immune regulation dimension. It provided a theoretical basis for the design of clinical protocols using combined immun checkpoint inhibitors.

3. Mutational inactivation of mTORC1 repressor gene DEPDC5 in human gastrointestinal stromal tumors (2019)#

Description: Published in PNAS, it reported for the first time that the mutant inactivation of the mTORC1 negative regulator gene DEPDC5 is an important driver of GIST progression (rather than initiation). This clarified a new molecular mechanism for the malignant progression of GIST after KIT/PDGFRA mutations.

Why it couldn’t be done before: DEPDC5 was previously mainly studied in neurological diseases such as focal epilepsy, and its association with GIST had not been discovered for a long time. Research on the progression mechanism of GIST requires large-scale genomic sequencing data. The large-scale application of next-generation sequencing in clinical tumor samples, combined with systematic research designs on the evolutionary trajectory of ‘micro-GIST→progressive GIST’, is a prerequisite for this discovery.

Impact: With 34 citations, it provided a new explanation for the progression mechanism after GIST targeted therapy resistance. The mTOR pathway thus entered the list of potential GIST treatment targets, with direct clinical translation value.

4. E3 ligase MKRN3 is a tumor suppressor regulating PABPC1 ubiquitination in non-small cell lung cancer (2021)#

Description: Published in Journal of Experimental Medicine, it was found that the central precocious puberty-causing gene MKRN3 is a true tumor suppressor in NSCLC. It regulates global protein synthesis by targeting PABPC1 ubiquitination modification, revealing a new tumor suppression axis of ‘E3 ligase-mRNA-binding protein-proteome homeostasis’.

Why it couldn’t be done before: MKRN3 was previously only recognized as a gene regulating puberty development. Its function in tumors was completely unknown. This discovery required a unique perspective of cross-correlating clinical central precocious puberty genetics with cancer genomic databases, as well as mature ubiquitomics protein profiling techniques to identify PABPC1 as a substrate. This cognitive leap from ’endocrine disease gene→tumor suppressor’ depends on the improvement of large-scale cancer genome atlas databases such as TCGA.

Impact: With 41 citations, it provided a new tumor suppression mechanism and potential treatment targets for NSCLC. It also pioneered a new paradigm of studying the functional reutilization of developmental genes in tumors.

5. CHIP-associated mutant ASXL1 in blood cells promotes solid tumor progression (2022)#

Description: It revealed that ASXL1 mutants related to cloning hematopoiesis (CHIP) can promote the progression of solid tumors. This established a causal relationship between cloned mutations in the blood system and tumors in non-hematopoietic systems, providing important evidence for CHIP’s cross-system impact on tumors.

Why it couldn’t be done before: The recognition of CHIP as an independent risk factor only emerged with large-scale population cohort studies from 2014 - 2017. To associate CHIP with solid tumor progression, precise CHIP mouse models, solid tumor transplantation systems, and a deep understanding of the functions of bone marrow-derived cells in the tumor microenvironment were required. These technologies and cognitive conditions matured around 2022.

Impact: With 30 citations, it extended CHIP research from cardiovascular diseases and blood malignancies to the field of solid tumors. It has important clinical significance for risk stratification and treatment strategy formulation for elderly cancer patients.

Research Directions#

  • Study on the functions of E3 ubiquitin ligases in tumors (STUB1/RUNX1, MKRN3/PABPC1)
  • Molecular drivers and new targets discovery for solid tumors (GIST, NSCLC, pancreatic cancer)
  • Role of microRNAs in cancer metastasis and EMT regulation
  • Cross-system impact of cloning hematopoiesis (CHIP) and tumor progression

Methodological Evolution#

In the early stage (2005 - 2016), research focused mainly on single molecular mechanisms. The classical cell line experiments + overexpression/knockout verification paradigm was used to study miRNA target gene regulation. From 2017 - 2019, a more integrated research strategy was adopted, introducing mouse in vivo models, drug intervention experiments, and single-cell immunotyping (CyTOF). This achieved a transition from ‘mechanism discovery’ to ’therapeutic validation’. A representative work is the AML research published in Nature Communications. From 2020 - 2022, it was further upgraded to a two-layer strategy of systematic screening + mechanism deep exploration: using CRISPR functional genomics for target screening or ubiquitomics mass spectrometry for target discovery (such as CDK1 screening, MKRN3-PABPC1 axis), followed by verification of clinical relevance with patient sample databases. The methodological maturity has been significantly improved.

Domain Impact#

The core contributions of this researcher in the field of molecular oncology are concentrated in three aspects: (1) It promoted the paradigm shift in AML targeted therapy from simple cell killing to a ’targeted + immune synergy’ model; (2) It systematically expanded the understanding of E3 ubiquitin ligases as tumor suppressor factors. In particular, the discovery of MKRN3 effectively connected developmental biology with oncology; (3) It provided new molecular targets and treatment strategies for refractory tumors such as GIST and NSCLC. Overall, this researcher is a stable contributor to the intersection of blood oncology and solid tumors. His publications are in high-level journals such as JEM, Nature Communications, PNAS, EMBO Molecular Medicine. However, it should be noted that his h-index of 12 is relatively low compared to the number of papers and top journals. This may be due to data distortion caused by aggregation of multiple researchers. The actual academic influence of a single researcher may be underestimated.
