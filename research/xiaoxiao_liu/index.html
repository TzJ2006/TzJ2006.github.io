Based on publication year, citation count, and thematic coherence, at least 3 distinct Xiaoxiao Liu researchers can be identified: (1) a molecular oncology researcher (accounting for the vast majority of the 451 citations, including the highest-cited miR-1271 pancreatic cancer paper with 62 citations and publications in Nature Communications/JEM/PNAS); (2) a computer architecture/image processing researcher (GPU memory design, hyperspectral denoising); (3) an LLM/AI security researcher (prompt engineering, safety evaluation in 2023–2025). The following analysis focuses primarily on the most representative and best-documented molecular oncology researcher, whose research trajectory shows a progressive deepening from microRNA regulation → p53-MDM2 targeted therapy → E3 ubiquitin ligase tumor suppression → CHIP clonal hematopoiesis.

Breakthrough Works#

1. miR-1271 inhibits migration, invasion and epithelial-mesenchymal transition by targeting ZEB1 and TWIST1 in pancreatic cancer cells (2016)#

Description: This work is the first systematic demonstration that miR-1271 inhibits pancreatic cancer cell migration, invasion, and epithelial-mesenchymal transition by simultaneously targeting two core EMT transcription factors, ZEB1 and TWIST1, providing a dual-target miRNA regulatory framework for pancreatic cancer metastasis mechanisms.

Why This Wasn’t Done Earlier: Prior to 2016, high-throughput miRNA sequencing costs were still prohibitive, and systematic studies of miRNA regulatory networks in pancreatic cancer were limited. The mechanism by which ZEB1 and TWIST1 could be co-targeted by the same miRNA had not yet been uncovered. It was only with the widespread adoption of NGS technology and the maturation of bioinformatics tools that this dual-target regulatory axis could be systematically screened and validated.

Impact: With 62 citations, this became the most-cited paper of this researcher, advancing the establishment of multi-target miRNA regulatory strategies in pancreatic cancer metastasis research and serving as an important reference for subsequent EMT-related therapeutic target studies.

2. Antitumor immunity augments the therapeutic effects of p53 activation on acute myeloid leukemia (2019)#

Description: Published in Nature Communications, this study demonstrated that the orally active p53-MDM2 interaction inhibitor DS-5272 not only exerts antitumor effects through direct activation of p53 signaling in AML cells, but also synergistically enhances the host’s antitumor immune response, revealing a cooperative mechanism between targeted therapy and tumor immunity.

Why This Wasn’t Done Earlier: p53-MDM2 inhibitors had long been thought to act exclusively through cell-autonomous pathways, and the long-standing separation between tumor immunology and targeted drug research made this synergistic relationship difficult to discover. Around 2019, the cross-disciplinary integration of immuno-oncology and small-molecule targeted therapy became a major research focus, and the maturation of single-cell immune profiling technologies such as CyTOF enabled precise quantification of immune cell subset dynamics.

Impact: 42 citations, published in Nature Communications. This work expanded AML p53-targeted therapy research from a single cytotoxic paradigm to include immune regulation, providing a theoretical basis for subsequent clinical regimen designs combining immune checkpoint inhibitors.

3. Mutational inactivation of mTORC1 repressor gene DEPDC5 in human gastrointestinal stromal tumors (2019)#

Description: Published in PNAS, this study was the first to report that mutational inactivation of the mTORC1 negative regulator DEPDC5 is an important driver of GIST progression (rather than initiation), elucidating a new molecular mechanism underlying malignant progression of GISTs following KIT/PDGFRA mutations.

Why This Wasn’t Done Earlier: DEPDC5 had previously been studied primarily in neurological disorders such as focal epilepsy, and its connection to GIST had long gone undiscovered. Investigating GIST progression mechanisms required large-scale genomic sequencing data; the widespread clinical application of next-generation sequencing in tumor samples, combined with a systematic study design examining the ‘micro-GIST → advanced GIST’ evolutionary trajectory, were necessary prerequisites for this discovery.

Impact: 34 citations. This study provided a new explanation for the mechanisms of GIST progression following targeted therapy resistance, bringing the mTOR pathway into the pool of GIST therapeutic target candidates with direct clinical translational value.

4. E3 ligase MKRN3 is a tumor suppressor regulating PABPC1 ubiquitination in non–small cell lung cancer (2021)#

Description: Published in the Journal of Experimental Medicine, this study revealed that MKRN3 — a gene previously known for causing central precocious puberty — is a bona fide tumor suppressor in NSCLC. It regulates global protein synthesis by targeting PABPC1 for ubiquitination, uncovering a completely novel ‘E3 ligase–mRNA-binding protein–proteostasis’ tumor suppression axis.

Why This Wasn’t Done Earlier: MKRN3 had previously been recognized only as a regulator of pubertal development, with no known function in cancer. This discovery required the unique insight of cross-referencing clinical central precocious puberty genetics with cancer genomics databases, as well as mature ubiquitomics proteomics technology to identify PABPC1 as a substrate. This conceptual leap from ‘developmental disease gene → tumor suppressor’ depended on the completeness of large-scale cancer genomics databases such as TCGA.

Impact: 41 citations. This work provided a novel tumor suppression mechanism and potential therapeutic target for NSCLC, while establishing a new paradigm for studying the functional repurposing of developmental genes in cancer.

5. CHIP‐associated mutant ASXL1 in blood cells promotes solid tumor progression (2022)#

Description: This study revealed that blood cells harboring CHIP-associated ASXL1 mutations can promote solid tumor progression, establishing a causal link between clonal mutations in the hematopoietic system and non-hematopoietic tumors, and representing important evidence for the cross-system tumor influence of CHIP.

Why This Wasn’t Done Earlier: The recognition of CHIP as an independent risk factor itself only emerged between 2014 and 2017 through large-scale population cohort studies. Linking CHIP to solid tumor progression required the simultaneous availability of precise CHIP mouse models, solid tumor transplantation systems, and a deep understanding of bone marrow-derived cell function in the tumor microenvironment — technical and conceptual prerequisites that only converged around 2022.

Impact: 30 citations. This study extended CHIP research from cardiovascular disease and hematologic malignancy risk into the realm of solid tumors, with important clinical implications for risk stratification and treatment strategy formulation in elderly cancer patients.

  • Functional roles of E3 ubiquitin ligases in tumors (STUB1/RUNX1, MKRN3/PABPC1)
  • Molecular driver mechanisms and novel target discovery in solid tumors (GIST, NSCLC, pancreatic cancer)
  • The role of microRNAs in cancer metastasis and EMT regulation
  • Cross-system effects of clonal hematopoiesis (CHIP) on tumor progression

Methodological Evolution#

The early phase (2005–2016) was dominated by single-molecule mechanism studies, employing the classic cell line experiment + overexpression/knockdown validation paradigm to investigate miRNA target gene regulation. From 2017 to 2019, research shifted toward integrative strategies, incorporating in vivo mouse models, drug intervention experiments, and immune cell profiling (CyTOF), achieving a leap from “mechanism discovery” to “therapeutic validation,” with the Nature Communications AML study as the flagship work. From 2020 to 2022, the approach was further upgraded to a two-tier strategy of systematic screening combined with deep mechanistic investigation: using CRISPR functional genomics screens or ubiquitomics mass spectrometry to identify targets (e.g., CDK1 screening, the MKRN3-PABPC1 axis), supplemented by patient sample database validation of clinical relevance, reflecting a significant increase in methodological sophistication.

Field Impact#

This researcher’s core contributions to molecular oncology are concentrated in three areas: (1) advancing the paradigm shift in AML targeted therapy from pure cytotoxic killing to a “targeted + immune synergy” model; (2) systematically expanding the understanding of E3 ubiquitin ligases as tumor suppressors in solid tumors, particularly through the discovery of MKRN3, which effectively bridges developmental biology and oncology; (3) providing new molecular targets and therapeutic strategies for refractory tumors such as GIST and NSCLC. Overall, this researcher is a steady contributor at the intersection of hematologic malignancies and solid tumors, with publications in high-impact journals including JEM, Nature Communications, PNAS, and EMBO Molecular Medicine.
