Landmark Works#

1. The dynamics of alcohol and marijuana initiation: patterns and predictors of first use in adolescence (2000)#

Description: Using longitudinal tracking data, this study systematically analyzed the temporal dynamics and predictors of adolescents’ first use of alcohol and marijuana, revealing developmental patterns in the initiation of substance use.

Why it was not done before: Required the accumulation of large-scale longitudinal cohort data and a sufficiently long tracking period spanning adolescence — data of this kind did not mature until the late 1990s. The study also demanded multilevel statistical modeling methods capable of handling repeated measures.

Impact: 444 citations; became a landmark reference in research on adolescent substance use initiation and provided a key predictive framework for subsequent intervention studies.

2. Long-Term Effects of the Seattle Social Development Intervention on School Bonding Trajectories (2001)#

Description: Evaluated the long-term effects of the Seattle Social Development Project by tracking the developmental trajectories of school bonding, demonstrating the lasting impact of early social intervention.

Why it was not done before: Intervention research requires more than a decade of follow-up to assess long-term outcomes. The high cost of data collection and the difficulty of controlling attrition rates meant that sufficient funding for such large-scale longitudinal intervention studies only became available in the 1990s.

Impact: 416 citations; established an empirical foundation for the effectiveness of early intervention in the field of prevention science, shaping youth health policy for decades.

3. Developmental relationships between adolescent substance use and risky sexual behavior in young adulthood (2002)#

Description: From a developmental psychology perspective, this study revealed the longitudinal associations between adolescent substance use and risky sexual behavior in early adulthood, proposing a co-developmental pathway hypothesis.

Why it was not done before: Required long-term tracking data spanning adolescence through early adulthood, with simultaneous measurement of multiple risk behaviors. The methodological tools for joint analysis of multiple risk behaviors — such as latent growth curve models — did not fully mature until the early 2000s.

Impact: 298 citations; effectively bridged the fields of substance abuse research and sexual health research, advancing the development of integrated adolescent risk behavior intervention strategies.

4. Family influences on the risk of daily smoking initiation (2005)#

Description: Systematically analyzed the pathways through which family factors — including parental smoking behavior, family monitoring, and parent-child relationship quality — influence adolescents’ risk of initiating daily smoking.

Why it was not done before: Required matched data collected from both adolescent self-reports and parental reports, while controlling for genetic confounders. The widespread adoption of survival analysis in epidemiology was a key technical prerequisite.

Impact: 289 citations; directly influenced the design of family-based smoking cessation interventions and has been widely cited in tobacco control policy research.

5. Superior thermal conductivity in suspended bilayer hexagonal boron nitride (2016)#

Description: Used suspended pre-patterned microstructures to measure the in-plane thermal conductivity of exfoliated bilayer hexagonal boron nitride, revealing its exceptional thermal transport properties.

Why it was not done before: Required dry-transfer techniques for preparing high-quality suspended h-BN samples with minimal polymer residue, as well as a precision thermal measurement platform for ultra-thin 2D materials — conditions that only became available around 2015.

Impact: Opened a research direction for hexagonal boron nitride as a thermal management material and established an experimental benchmark for understanding heat transport physics in 2D materials.
