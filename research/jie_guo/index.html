Breakthrough Works#

1. The dynamics of alcohol and marijuana initiation: patterns and predictors of first use in adolescence (2000)#

Description: Using longitudinal tracking data, this study analyzed the timing and predictors of adolescents’ first use of alcohol and marijuana, revealing the developmental patterns of substance abuse initiation.

Why it couldn’t be done before: It required large-scale longitudinal cohort data and a long tracking period (spanning adolescence). Such data became available only in the late 1990s. Additionally, multi-level statistical modeling methods were needed to handle repeated measurements.

Impact: With 444 citations, this paper became a classic literature on adolescent substance abuse initiation, providing a framework of key predictors for subsequent intervention studies.

2. Long-Term Effects of the Seattle Social Development Intervention on School Bonding Trajectories (2001)#

Description: This study evaluated the long-term effects of the Seattle social development intervention project, tracking the development of school belonging and proving the lasting impact of early social interventions.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Interventional studies require a follow-up period of over 10 years to assess long-term effects. Data collection was costly, and controlling for dropout rates was difficult. Such large-scale longitudinal interventions were only supported by funding in the 1990s.

Impact: With 416 citations, this paper laid an empirical foundation for early intervention effectiveness in the field of prevention science, influencing adolescent health policies for decades.

3. Developmental relationships between adolescent substance use and risky sexual behavior in young adulthood (2002)#

Description: From a developmental psychology perspective, this study revealed the longitudinal relationship between adolescent substance use and high-risk sexual behavior in early adulthood, proposing a common development path hypothesis.

Why it couldn’t be done before: It required long-term tracking data from adolescence to early adulthood, with measurements of multiple risk behaviors. The methodology for analyzing such multi-risk behavior combinations (such as latent variable growth curve models) became more mature only in the early 2000s.

Impact: With 298 citations, this paper effectively connected substance abuse research with sexual health research, promoting the development of integrated adolescent risk behavior intervention strategies.

4. Family influences on the risk of daily smoking initiation (2005)#

Description: This study systematically analyzed the impact of family factors (parental smoking behavior, family supervision, quality parent-child relationships) on adolescents’ risk of starting to smoke daily.

Why it couldn’t be done before: It required collecting matching data reported by teenagers and their parents simultaneously, while controlling for genetic confounding factors. The widespread use of survival analysis methods in epidemiology was a technical prerequisite.

Impact: With 289 citations, this paper directly influenced the design of tobacco control intervention projects at the family level and was widely cited in tobacco control policy research.

5. Superior thermal conductivity in suspended bilayer hexagonal boron nitride (2016)#

Description: This study measured the surface thermal conductivity of suspended bilayer hexagonal boron nitride using pre-patterned microstructures, revealing its extremely high thermal conductivity properties.

Why it couldn’t be done before: It required dry transfer technology to prepare high-quality suspended h-BN samples (reducing polymer residues) and a precise thermal measurement platform for ultra-thin two-dimensional materials. These technologies became available around 2015.

Impact: This study opened up new research directions for hexagonal boron nitride as a thermal management material, providing an experimental basis for understanding the physics of heat transfer in two-dimensional materials.
