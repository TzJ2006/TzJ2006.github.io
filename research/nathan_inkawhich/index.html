Research Track#

Nathan Inkawhich is a researcher focusing on the intersection of adversarial machine learning and SAR (Synthetic Aperture Radar) remote sensing recognition. Since 2017, he has been developing in two parallel directions: defense/remote sensing applications and deep learning security. His academic breakthrough occurred between 2019-2020, when his papers in CVPR, NeurIPS (two papers each), and ICLR established him as a leader in the field of transferable adversarial attacks. Four papers published in top journals received over 500 citations, marking a crucial period in his academic career.

Since 2021, he has made a systematic shift in his research focus: from “how to create stronger attacks” to “how to build more reliable recognition systems”. His work focuses on open-world SAR-ATR (Automatic Target Recognition), out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, and multi-modal remote sensing image understanding. This has led to the development of a core approach for “safe and reliable AI recognition systems”. Since 2023, he has been systematically evaluating the applicability and vulnerabilities of basic models (such as CLIP, DINOv2) in adversarial attacks and SAR recognition. His research scope has expanded from attack-defense mechanisms to practical evaluation of basic models.

Breakthrough Works#

1. Feature Space Perturbations Yield More Transferable Adversarial Examples (2019)#

Description: Proposed a method for black-box transferable adversarial attacks based on deep feature space perturbations. Unlike previous mainstream methods that applied perturbations to the output layer (softmax/logit layers) to directly manipulate classification results, this work applies perturbations to intermediate feature layers. This significantly improves the success rate of target-oriented adversarial samples in black-box scenarios and provides interpretable analysis of the model’s internal representation.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Previous research generally believed that adversarial perturbations should be applied to the output layer (softmax/logit layers) to directly control classification results. There was a lack of systematic understanding of the semantic structure of intermediate feature layers. The key insight was that researchers didn’t realize that the class separability structure of feature layers was the fundamental source of transferability, rather than the decision boundary of the output layer. This work systematically linked feature distribution modeling with the mechanism of transferability for the first time.

Impact: Covered 199 citations and became an important reference baseline for subsequent research on transferable adversarial attacks. It promoted a shift from the “output layer attack” paradigm to the “feature layer attack” paradigm, and inspired a series of subsequent studies based on intermediate layer representations in attack-defense scenarios.

2. Transferable Perturbations of Deep Feature Distributions (2020)#

Description: By modeling the class-level distribution (mean and covariance) of deep features, a method for adversarial attacks was designed that completely separated from output layer information. This achieved the best performance among target-oriented black-box transfer attacks at that time.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Previous feature layer attack methods usually only targeted individual feature activation points, rather than statistically modeling the overall feature distribution. Accurate estimation of class-condition feature distributions required a large number of similar samples and corresponding distribution modeling techniques. This idea was not introduced into adversarial attack design at that time. Computing power wasn’t the bottleneck; the key was introducing the statistical perspective of “feature distribution”.

Impact: Covered 98 citations (published in ICLR). It introduced statistical distribution modeling into adversarial attack design, providing a methodological basis for subsequent generative adversarial samples and feature-matching-based attack methods.

3. Perturbing Across the Feature Hierarchy to Improve Standard and Strict Blackbox Attack Transferability (2020)#

Description: Proposed a method for cross-layer feature hierarchy perturbations (PAH). By applying perturbations to multiple feature layers and weighting them together, this method improved the transferability of standard black-box attacks. It also maintained effectiveness under stricter black-box constraints (where the source model and target model had no overlapping labeled spaces).

Why it couldn’t be done before: Previous work usually only targeted a single feature layer for attacks, lacking an understanding that “different levels of features contain complementary adversarial information”. Multi-layer joint optimization faced engineering challenges in designing optimization objectives and gradient balancing. This work solved the problem of layer-weighting distribution through systematic ablation experiments.

Impact: Covered 100 citations (NeurIPS 2020). It complemented the ICLR paper from the same year in terms of methodology, forming a complete theoretical system for Inkawhich’s research on feature-layer adversarial attacks.

4. DVERGE: Diversifying Vulnerabilities for Enhanced Robust Generation of Ensembles (2020)#

Description: Identified overlapping adversarial vulnerabilities in CNN integrated models (different models failed to work with the same input). The DVERGE method enhances the overall robustness of integration by actively diversifying the adversarial vulnerabilities of each sub-model, making it difficult for attacks targeting a single sub-model to be transferred to the entire integration.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Integrated learning has long been seen as a natural solution for adversarial robustness. However, the core problem of “vulnerability overlap” had not been systematically quantified and studied before. It was necessary to establish a theoretical understanding of transferable adversarial attacks (which is exactly Inkawhich’s previous work) before using this mechanism to design diversified training objectives.

Impact: Covered 128 citations (NeurIPS 2020). It transformed the theoretical understanding of adversarial transferability into defensive design principles, opening up research directions for integrated robustness based on “vulnerability diversification”.

5. Bridging a Gap in SAR-ATR: Training on Fully Synthetic and Testing on Measured Data (2021)#

Description: Systematically studied the domain adaptation problem between fully synthetic SAR data training and real measurement data testing (Sim-to-Real gap). Proposed a transfer learning framework for SAR-ATR, proving that synthetic data could effectively support model training in defense scenarios where labeled real data was extremely scarce.

Why it couldn’t be done before: The computational cost of generating synthetic SAR data (electromagnetic scattering simulation) dropped significantly in the late 2010s. At the same time, deep domain adaptation methods became more mature. The combination of both made the Sim-to-Real approach feasible for SAR-ATR. Previous problems were a double bottleneck: insufficient data (very few real SAR labeled samples) and low simulation accuracy.

Impact: Covered 97 citations. It provided a practical path for the defense/remote sensing community to train SAR-ATR models with low data costs, making it a benchmark work widely cited in this field.

Research Directions#

  • Transferable black-box adversarial attacks (based on feature layer distribution modeling)
  • SAR automatic target recognition (SAR-ATR) and open-world robustness
  • Out-of-distribution detection (OOD) and fine-grained recognition
  • Multi-modal remote sensing image understanding (SAR + EO fusion)
  • Evaluation of basic models’ applicability in adversarial scenarios and SAR domains

Methodological Evolution#

Inkawhich’s methodological evolution went through three clear stages. The first stage (2018-2020): Focused on “attack” as the core, starting from pixel-level adversarial perturbations and gradually moving to feature layers. First, single-layer feature mean perturbation was used (CVPR 2019), then class feature distribution modeling was introduced (ICLR 2020), and finally cross-layer feature hierarchy perturbations were developed (NeurIPS 2020). This formed a complete theoretical system for feature space adversarial attacks. DVERGE reversed the attack mechanism into a defensive tool, completing an attack-defense loop.

The second stage (2021-2023): The methodology shifted from “creating adversarial samples” to “building reliable recognition systems”. In the field of SAR-ATR, Sim-to-Real transfer learning and few-shot learning were introduced. In OOD detection, data augmentation strategies like Mixup Outlier Exposure were used, and fine-grained and hierarchical OOD inference was explored. The method design paid more attention to practical scenario constraints (data scarcity, open-world scenarios, deployment environment differences). The third stage (2023-to-date): Shifted to a systematic evaluation perspective, conducting comprehensive benchmark tests on the adversarial robustness and SAR applicability of basic models like CLIP/DINOv2. The methodology evolved from “proposing new methods” to “establishing evaluation frameworks and understanding boundaries”.

Domain Impact#

Inkawhich’s core contributions to the field were concentrated at two intersections: one was introducing statistical feature distribution modeling into transferable adversarial attack design, which promoted a deeper understanding of the mechanism behind “why adversarial samples can be transferred across models”. His four papers published in top journals between 2019-2020 received over 520 citations, making it an important reference document for this sub-field. The other contribution was systematic work on the practical application of SAR intelligent recognition, covering core challenges like Sim-to-Real transfer learning, few-shot learning, open-world OOD detection, and multi-modal fusion. This had significant practical value for the defense/remote sensing AI community. Overall, he is a leading researcher with distinct domain characteristics: he established a unique research identity in the intersection of adversarial ML and remote sensing recognition. However, his influence was mainly concentrated in specific sub-fields, and he hasn’t achieved widespread influence that would establish himself as a foundational figure in the overall field.
