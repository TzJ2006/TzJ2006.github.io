Research Trajectory#

Linghao Song is a computer architecture researcher whose trajectory consistently centers on in/near-memory computing. In 2017, his ReRAM-based graph processing accelerator GraphR won a Best Paper Award, establishing his early reputation in non-volatile memory computing. His most prolific period (2020–2022) saw him systematically build a complete research ecosystem around sparse linear algebra acceleration (SpMV, SpMM, conjugate gradient solvers) and FPGA high-level synthesis frameworks (TAPA). In 2023, NDSEARCH appeared at ISCA — a top computer architecture venue — adapting near-data processing to vector databases and LLM retrieval-augmented generation, completing a pivotal transition from traditional HPC to AI systems.

A defining characteristic of his trajectory is a dual-track “tooling + applications” approach: the TAPA/RapidStream-IR series provides FPGA programming infrastructure, while the Serpens/Sextans/NDSEARCH series represents high-impact applications built on that infrastructure. This in-house toolchain strategy gives his research strong reproducibility and community reach. Note that two cosmology/neutrino papers from 2024 (related to the Seesaw mechanism) are entirely disconnected from the rest of his work and most likely belong to a different researcher with the same name; they are excluded from this analysis.

Breakthrough Works#

1. GraphR: Accelerating Graph Processing Using ReRAM (2017)#

Description: Proposed the first ReRAM-based graph processing accelerator, mapping graph adjacency matrices directly onto ReRAM crossbar arrays and using analog circuits to perform massively parallel matrix-vector multiplication — bypassing the memory bandwidth bottleneck of traditional von Neumann architectures and achieving extremely energy-efficient graph computation.

Why It Wasn’t Done Before: Before 2015, ReRAM device fabrication was not mature enough — device-to-device variability was too high for precise conductance control. More importantly, the key insight was missing: prior researchers treated ReRAM purely as a digital storage replacement. GraphR was the first to systematically argue that graph computation is highly tolerant of numerical imprecision and that sparse matrices are naturally suited to analog-domain parallelism, making analog errors acceptable.

Impact: Pioneered the “ReRAM as a compute substrate” research direction. The Best Paper Award cemented it as a landmark work in near-memory computing, directly spawning a large body of follow-on ReRAM accelerator research. Its methodology — mapping sparse matrices onto analog arrays — has been widely cited and extended.

2. Refloat: Low-Cost Floating-Point Processing in ReRAM for Accelerating Iterative Linear Solvers (2020)#

Description: Tackled the central engineering challenge in ReRAM analog computing: floating-point support. The work proposes decomposing floating-point operations into forms that can be executed at low cost in ReRAM, applied to iterative linear solvers (a core operator in scientific computing), and was published at SC, the top supercomputing venue.

Why It Wasn’t Done Before: Floating-point operations demand high precision (IEEE 754 single-precision requires a 23-bit mantissa), yet ReRAM analog computation is inherently noisy. The prevailing assumption was that “ReRAM is only suitable for low-precision integer/fixed-point arithmetic.” The missing insight was that iterative solvers in scientific computing have self-convergent properties, allowing intermediate steps to use mixed precision. Refloat found a controllable trade-off between precision error and convergence speed — a conceptual breakthrough, not merely an engineering refinement.

Impact: Extended the applicability of ReRAM acceleration from graph and neural network workloads to scientific computing, opening up a new HPC+ReRAM research space. Its 11 citations represent solid performance for an SC paper.

3. TAPA: A Scalable Task-Parallel Dataflow Programming Framework for Modern FPGAs with Co-Optimization of HLS and Physical Design (2022)#

Description: Proposed TAPA, an end-to-end compilation framework that allows users to describe computation as task-parallel dataflow programs in C++, automatically compiling them into high-frequency FPGA accelerators. The key innovation is the co-optimization of high-level synthesis (HLS) and physical floorplanning, addressing the long-standing problem of frequency collapse in large-scale FPGA designs.

Why It Wasn’t Done Before: Mainstream tools such as Xilinx Vitis HLS treated HLS and physical implementation as separate stages, causing the logic generated by HLS to suffer severe frequency degradation after place-and-route. The missing insight was that task-parallel granularity naturally aligns with the physical partitioning of FPGA SLRs (Super Logic Regions) — if physical constraints are made visible at the HLS stage, high productivity and high performance can be achieved simultaneously. This requires deep understanding of both FPGA architecture and HLS internals, and exemplifies cross-layer abstraction capability.

Impact: Became an important infrastructure tool for academic FPGA accelerator research. TAPA-CS subsequently extended its reach to distributed multi-FPGA scenarios, lowering the barrier to developing high-performance FPGA accelerators.

4. NDSEARCH: Accelerating Graph-Traversal-Based Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search through Near Data Processing (2023)#

Description: Proposed a near-data processing acceleration solution for graph-traversal-based approximate nearest neighbor search (ANNS, e.g., HNSW/NSG algorithms) in vector databases, published at ISCA (a top computer architecture venue), directly targeting the retrieval engine underpinning LLM retrieval-augmented generation (RAG).

Why It Wasn’t Done Before: Before 2022, ANNS graph-traversal algorithms were treated primarily as software/algorithmic problems. The explosive growth of LLM+RAG in 2022–2023 was what made ANNS a critical datacenter workload, creating strong motivation for hardware acceleration. On the architecture side, there was previously insufficient empirical evidence to demonstrate that ANNS is memory-bandwidth-bound rather than compute-bound; NDSEARCH provided the key evidence through profiling, making the near-data processing approach compelling.

Impact: With 22 citations, this is his most-cited work to date, arriving at precisely the right moment in the LLM infrastructure wave. It redirected the architecture community’s attention toward vector database workloads within the near-memory computing space, spurring a substantial body of follow-on ANNS accelerator research.

Research Areas#

  • Near/in-memory computing and hardware accelerator design (ReRAM, HBM, NDP)
  • Sparse linear algebra and graph computation acceleration (SpMV, SpMM, graph traversal)
  • FPGA high-level synthesis and programming frameworks (TAPA, RapidStream)
  • System acceleration for AI/LLM infrastructure (ANNS, vector databases, RAG)

Methodological Evolution#

In the early phase (2017–2019), the dominant paradigm was analog computing — exploiting ReRAM’s physical properties for matrix operations, with the methodological core being a precision-versus-efficiency trade-off analysis. From 2020–2021, the focus shifted to digital sparse accelerator design; the methodology evolved into “dataflow analysis + memory access pattern modeling,” addressing load imbalance and irregular memory access in sparse computation. The release of the Pyxis dataset also reflects a growing emphasis on rigorous evaluation methodology. In 2022, a “cross-layer co-optimization” methodology was introduced: TAPA simultaneously spans the programming model, HLS compiler, and physical design layers, signaling an expansion of research scope from individual accelerators to complete toolchains. By 2023, the methodology had further evolved toward “workload-driven architecture design”: NDSEARCH begins with performance bottleneck analysis of real LLM application scenarios and works backward to hardware architecture decisions, reflecting a mature system research paradigm anchored in application requirements.

Overall, the methodological evolution follows the path: analog near-memory computing → digital sparse acceleration → compiler–hardware co-design → AI-application-driven architecture. The persistent thread throughout is a sustained focus on the memory bandwidth bottleneck; the means of addressing it have simply evolved from analog computation to HBM utilization to near-data processing.

Field Impact#

Linghao Song’s contributions to computer architecture span two dimensions. The first is directional impact: GraphR (2017 Best Paper) and NDSEARCH (ISCA 2023) each sent a guiding research signal at a pivotal moment — the former inspiring a generation of ReRAM accelerator work, the latter arriving at the peak of LLM infrastructure interest and triggering broad follow-up. The second is infrastructure impact: the TAPA framework, used as an open-source tool, has lowered the barrier to entry for FPGA accelerator research and produced a multiplier effect. Looking at citation distribution, NDSEARCH’s 22 citations accumulated within just one to two years of its 2023 publication, indicating strong recent influence at the intersection of AI systems and computer architecture — closely aligned with current LLM infrastructure research trends.
