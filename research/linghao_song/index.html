Research Trajectory#

Linghao Song is a researcher specializing in computer architecture. His research trajectory focuses on memory-based computing. In 2017, he won the Best Paper Award for his work on ReRAM graph processing accelerator GraphR, which laid the foundation for his early reputation in non-volatile memory computing. From 2020 to 2022, he achieved peak performance, systematically developing a complete research ecosystem around sparse linear algebra acceleration (SpMV, SpMM, conjugate gradient solvers) and FPGA high-level synthesis framework (TAPA). In 2023, his paper NDSEARCH was published in the computer architecture conference ISCA. It transformed data processing paradigms into vector databases and LLM retrieval-enhanced generation scenarios, completing a key transition from traditional HPC to AI systems.

The key feature of his research trajectory is the simultaneous advancement of “tools + applications”. The TAPA/RapidStream-IR series provides FPGA programming infrastructure, while the Serpens/Sextans/NDSEARCH series are high-impact applications driven by this infrastructure. This self-developed toolchain gives his research strong reproducibility and community influence. It’s worth noting that two papers on cosmology/neutrino in 2024, related to the Seesaw mechanism, were completely unrelated to other research directions. They likely belonged to researchers with the same name but different identities, so they were not included in this trajectory analysis.

Breakthrough Works#

1. GraphR: Accelerating Graph Processing Using ReRAM (2017)#

Description: Proposed the first graph processing accelerator based on ReRAM resistive memory. The adjacency matrix of a graph was directly mapped to the ReRAM cross-array, and analog circuits were used to perform large-scale parallel matrix-vector multiplication. This allowed bypassing the memory bandwidth bottleneck in the traditional Von Neumann architecture, achieving extremely low energy consumption for graph computing.

Why it couldn’t be done before: ReRAM devices had insufficient technology maturity before 2015, with high variability between devices, making it difficult to accurately control conductivity values. The key insight was that researchers previously regarded ReRAM as a substitute for digital storage. However, GraphR systematically demonstrated the core compatibility of “graph computing being tolerant of high precision, and sparse matrices being naturally suitable for parallel simulation domains”, making simulation errors acceptable.

Impact: It initiated the research direction of “ReRAM as a computing base”. The Best Paper Award made it one of the landmark works in near-memory computing. It directly inspired many subsequent ReRAM accelerator studies. The methodology (mapping sparse matrices to analog arrays) was widely cited and extended.

2. Refloat: Low-Cost Floating-Point Processing in ReRAM for Accelerating Iterative Linear Solvers (2020)#

Description: Solved the most critical engineering problem in ReRAM simulation computing: floating-point support. It proposed decomposing floating-point operations into forms that could be executed at low cost in ReRAM and applied to iterative linear solvers, which are core operators in scientific computing. This work was published in the top computer conference SC.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Floating-point operations require high precision (IEEE 754 single-precision requires 23 bits of mantissa). However, ReRAM simulation calculations naturally have noise. It was previously believed that “ReRAM is only suitable for low-precision integer/fixed-point operations”. The key insight was that iterative solvers in scientific computing have self-convergence characteristics, allowing intermediate steps to use mixed precision. Refloat found a controllable balance between precision error and convergence speed. This was a cognitive breakthrough rather than just an engineering improvement.

Impact: It expanded the scope of ReRAM acceleration from graph/neural networks to scientific computing, opening up new research areas for HPC+ReRAM. With 11 citations, it showed a solid performance in SC papers.

3. TAPA: A Scalable Task-Parallel Dataflow Programming Framework for Modern FPGAs with Co-Optimization of HLS and Physical Design (2022)#

Description: Proposed a end-to-end compilation framework called TAPA, which allows users to describe computations using C++ task parallel dataflow programs. It automatically compiles into high-frequency FPGA accelerators. The key innovation was the simultaneous optimization of high-level synthesis (HLS) and physical layout, solving the problem of frequency collapse in large-scale FPGA designs.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Mainstream tools like Xilinx Vitis HLS considered HLS and physical implementation as separate stages, resulting in a significant decrease in frequency of logic generated by HLS after layout and routing. The key insight was that task parallel granularity naturally aligned with the SLR (Super Logic Region) physical partitions of FPGA. By recognizing physical constraints during the HLS stage, high productivity and performance could be achieved simultaneously. This required deep understanding of both FPGA architecture and HLS internal mechanisms, reflecting cross-layer abstraction capabilities.

Impact: It became an important basic construction tool for FPGA accelerator research in academia. Later, TAPA-CS was further extended to distributed multi-FPGA scenarios. It reduced the development threshold for high-performance FPGA accelerators.

4. NDSEARCH: Accelerating Graph-Traversal-Based Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search through Near Data Processing (2023)#

Description: Proposed a near-data processing acceleration scheme for approximate nearest neighbor search based on graph traversal in vector databases, such as HNSW/NSG algorithms. This work was published in the computer architecture conference ISCA, directly targeting the underlying indexing core of LLM retrieval-enhanced generation (RAG).

Why it couldn’t be done before: Graph traversal algorithms for ANNS were mainly regarded as software/algorithms problems before 2022. It was only during 2022-2023 that the explosive growth of LLM+RAG made ANNS a critical load in data centers, giving rise to strong motivation for hardware acceleration. In terms of computing power, there was a lack of sufficient empirical data to prove that ANNS was a memory bandwidth bottleneck rather than a computational bottleneck. NDSEARCH provided key evidence through profiling, making the near-data processing scheme persuasive.

Impact: With 22 citations, it shows to be one of the most cited works so far. It happened to coincide with the time when LLM infrastructure research was in full swing. It directed the attention of the architecture community towards the vector database track, driving a large number of ANNS accelerator studies later on.

Research Directions#

  • Near-memory/in-memory computing and hardware accelerator design (ReRAM, HBM, NDP)
  • Sparse linear algebra and graph computing acceleration (SpMV, SpMM, graph traversal)
  • FPGA high-level synthesis and programming frameworks (TAPA, RapidStream)
  • System acceleration for AI/LLM infrastructure (ANNS, vector databases, RAG)

Methodology Evolution#

In the early stage (2017-2019), simulation-based computing paradigms were used. ReRAM physical properties were utilized to perform matrix operations. The core methodology was the analysis of the trade-off between “precision and energy efficiency”. From 2020 to 2021, the methodology shifted towards sparse accelerator design in the digital domain. The methodology was upgraded to “dataflow analysis + memory access pattern modeling”, focusing on solving problems such as load imbalance and random memory access in sparse computing. The release of Pyxis dataset also reflected the emphasis on system evaluation methodologies. In 2022, the methodology of “cross-layer collaborative optimization” was introduced. TAPA simultaneously optimized three levels: programming model, HLS compiler, and physical design. This marked a shift from single accelerator to a complete toolchain. In 2023, the methodology further evolved towards “workload-driven architecture design”. NDSEARCH started by analyzing performance bottlenecks based on real LLM application scenarios, then derived hardware architecture decisions, reflecting a mature system research paradigm centered around application requirements.

Overall, the evolution path of methodology is: simulation-based near-memory computing → digital sparse acceleration → compiler and hardware collaboration → AI-application-driven architecture. The continuous focus was on memory bandwidth bottlenecks, with solutions evolving from simulation computing to HBM utilization and then to near-data processing.

Domain Impact#

Linghao Song’s contributions in the field of computer architecture are reflected in two dimensions: one is directional contributions. GraphR (2017 Best Paper) and NDSEARCH (ISCA 2023) sent guiding research signals at two key moments: ReRAM graph acceleration and vector database acceleration. The former inspired a generation of ReRAM accelerator work, while the latter coincided with the LLM infrastructure boom, leading to widespread follow-up efforts. The second dimension is infrastructure contributions. The TAPA framework was used as an open-source tool, reducing the entry barrier for FPGA accelerator research. This had a multiplier effect. From the citation distribution, NDSEARCH’s 22 citations were concentrated within just one year after its publication in 2023, indicating its strong short-term influence in the intersection of AI systems and architecture. This is highly consistent with current LLM infrastructure research hotspots.
