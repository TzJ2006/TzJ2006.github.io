Research Trajectory#

The “Fan Chen” in this dataset is likely a combined record of multiple researchers with the same name, not a complete trajectory of a single scholar. From the content of his papers, at least two distinct types of researchers can be identified: (1) A researcher who has been engaged in analytical chemistry/food chemistry research since 1992, with his work evolving from forensic gas chromatography in the early days to ion liquid extraction and natural deep eutectic solvents (NADES); (2) A group of computer science papers published after 2019, focusing on topics such as reinforcement learning, ReRAM accelerators, quantum computing, optimization theory, diffusion models, etc. These papers have nothing to do with the previous research and clearly belong to different groups of researchers.

In terms of the main area of analytical chemistry, his academic trajectory is clear: from 1992 to 2008, he focused on forensic toxicological gas chromatography detection. In the 2010s, he turned to HPLC-DAD food/drug fingerprinting. In 2015-2016, he made a significant breakthrough by introducing ion liquids into liquid microextraction and countercurrent chromatography, which was published in Food Chemistry and J. Chromatography A, with 85 and 61 citations respectively. From 2019 to 2021, he explored the field of green extraction using natural deep eutectic solvents (NADES), publishing several high-citation papers and establishing himself as an academic leader in this niche area of green analytical chemistry.

His h-index of 12, total citations of 542, and the ratio of 287 citations in the last 5 years indicate that he is a mid-level researcher whose influence continues to grow in a specific niche area, such as ion liquids/NADES for green pretreatment, rather than a cross-disciplinary expert.

Breakthrough Works#

1. Determination of chlorophenols in honey samples using in-situ ionic liquid-dispersive liquid-liquid microextraction as a pretreatment method followed by high-performance liquid chromatography (2015)#

Description: Proposed an in-situ ionic liquid dispersion liquid-liquid microextraction method. By using hydrophilic ionic liquid [C4MIM][BF4] to react with anion exchange to generate hydrophobic extractant [C4MIM][NTf2], it was possible to enrich six chlorophenols in honey and detect them using HPLC. This method was cited 85 times.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Traditional DLLME relied on toxic organic solvents like chlorobenzene and carbon tetrachloride as extractants. There were many interfering substances in food matrices, and the strategy of generating ionic liquids in situ had not been systematically applied to the analysis of trace pollutants in honey before. Additionally, the cost of commercial ionic liquids decreased significantly in the late 2010s, making this method feasible.

Impact: It opened up a practical path for ionic liquid microextraction in the field of food safety. Many subsequent papers followed this approach, extending it to complex matrices like wine and vegetable oils, becoming a landmark paper in this niche area.

2. Determination of Alternaria mycotoxins in wine and juice using ionic liquid modified countercurrent chromatography as a pretreatment method followed by high-performance liquid chromatography (2016)#

Description: Introduced ionic liquids into the countercurrent chromatography solvent system to achieve preparatory enrichment and purification of Alternaria mycotoxins in wine and juice. This work was published in J. Chromatography A and cited 61 times.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Countercurrent chromatography traditionally relied on standard solvent systems like n-hexane/ethyl acetate/methanol/water. Its selectivity for polar mycotoxins was limited. The adjustable polarity of ionic liquids had not been systematically integrated into countercurrent chromatography systems before 2015, and the low purity of ionic liquids in the early days led to unstable phase separation.

Impact: It demonstrated the feasibility of ionic liquid-modified countercurrent chromatography as a pretreatment tool for natural products and food contaminants. This led to a series of related works involving oil separation and anthocyanin purification.

3. Selective microextraction of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons using a hydrophobic deep eutectic solvent composed with an iron oxide-based nanoferrofluid (2019)#

Description: Combined hydrophobic deep eutectic solvents with iron oxide nanofluids to achieve magnetic-assisted selective microextraction of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. This work was published in Microchimica Acta and cited 40 times.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Hydrophobic NADES wasn’t systematically synthesized and characterized until around 2017. Combining DES with magnetic nanoparticles to create “magnetic DLLME” required both DES design and nanofluid preparation techniques, which were rarely explored before 2019.

Impact: It promoted the application of DES in environmental pollutant detection. This solution, which combines green solvents and magnetic separation, was widely used in subsequent research.

4. A priori design of new natural deep eutectic solvent for lutein recovery from microalgae (2021)#

Description: Based on a prior design framework for intermolecular hydrogen bonding interactions, this study systematically screened NADES for green extraction of microalgae lutein. This work was published in Food Chemistry and cited 74 times.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Previous NADES research relied heavily on trial-and-error experiments, lacking rational design methods based on molecular structure prediction of solubility. The combination of molecular dynamics simulations and Hansen solubility parameters in food extraction NADES design didn’t become mature until the early 2020s.

Impact: It shifted NADES research from empirical synthesis to prior design driven by mechanisms, providing a methodological example for green extraction chemistry. This had a direct impact on the direction of microalgae biorefinery.

5. A Theoretical Study on Terpene‐Based Natural Deep Eutectic Solvent: Relationship between Viscosity and Hydrogen‐Bonding Interactions (2021)#

Description: Through quantum chemistry calculations and molecular dynamics simulations, this study explained the microscopic mechanism behind the low viscosity of terpene-based NADES. The relationship between viscosity and hydrogen bonding interactions was quantified. This work was published in Global Challenges and cited 72 times.

Why it couldn’t be done before: High viscosity was a major obstacle to the industrialization of NADES, but its microscopic causes were unclear for a long time. Terpene-based low viscosity systems hadn’t been studied systematically before 2019. Systematically linking DFT calculations with rheological measurements required interdisciplinary integration between computational chemistry and analytical chemistry.

Impact: It theoretically explained the viscosity advantages of terpene-based NADES, providing a thermodynamic/dynamic basis for the directed design of low-viscosity green solvents. This promoted the development of such solvents for practical separation applications.

Methodological Evolution#

The methodological evolution of this analytical chemist shows a clear three-stage transition. In the first stage (1992–2008), gas chromatography (GC/FPD, GC-MS) was the core tool, focusing on quantitative detection of forensic toxins and food components. The methods were limited to single components and matrices, with limited technical depth. In the second stage (2010–2014), multi-component simultaneous determination using HPLC-DAD/ELSD became common, and attempts were made to build traditional Chinese medicine fingerprinting systems. However, technological innovation was still limited.

The third stage (since 2015) represents a fundamental paradigm shift: researchers introduced ion liquids and deep eutectic solvents into sample pretreatment processes. The core innovation shifted from “detection methods themselves” to “green and efficient sample enrichment/separation strategies.” This shift aligns with the overall trend of “green analytical chemistry” in the field of analytical chemistry, upgrading the work from technology application to method innovation, resulting in a significant increase in citations. More recently (2021), quantum chemistry calculations and molecular dynamics simulations were introduced, elevating empirical solvent design to rationally designed methods. This marks a higher level of methodological evolution towards computationally assisted experiments.
