Research Trajectory#

The academic trajectory of Xiongfei Yu exhibits a rare multi-phase cross-disciplinary development pattern, likely representing the aggregated works of multiple researchers sharing the same name. The first phase (1996–2004) centered on petroleum engineering in China, focusing on gas condensate prediction in gas reservoirs, CO₂ injection for enhanced oil recovery (EOR), and reservoir screening for gas injection — with a distinctly Chinese oilfield (Liaohe, Daqing, etc.) engineering background. The second phase (2005–2011) saw a dramatic shift toward geotechnical and civil engineering, with publications on 3D finite element analysis of dam stability, levee systems in Louisiana, and pile foundation testing in Georgia — work characteristic of North American engineering practice that bears almost no connection to petroleum engineering. The third phase (2017–2019) marks a return to the petroleum domain, but at a higher research level: rock physics and unconventional reservoir mechanics — focused on compaction and sand production effects on fluid flow in unconsolidated sandstones, and NMR spontaneous imbibition in tight sandstones — mirroring the wave of large-scale shale/tight oil development in China. The most-cited paper (2019, 108 citations) represents the apex of this researcher’s academic impact.

Landmark Works#

1. New insights into spontaneous imbibition in tight oil sandstones with NMR (2019)#

Description: Using nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) technology, this work systematically elucidated the microscopic mechanisms and quantitative patterns of capillary-driven spontaneous imbibition in tight sandstone reservoirs, providing experimental evidence for the oil production mechanism during post-fracturing shut-in periods, and directly guiding the optimization of fracturing fluid flowback and shut-in schedules in tight oil reservoirs.

Why it wasn’t done before: Pore throats in tight sandstones are extremely small (nanoscale), and conventional mercury intrusion and core experiments could not characterize fluid distribution and migration in situ and dynamically. The maturation of NMR core analysis technology and the widespread availability of high-field instruments provided the prerequisite for such research. Furthermore, China’s large-scale commercial development of shale/tight oil (post-2015) created urgent engineering demand, which in turn secured experimental funding and sample access.

Impact: With 108 citations, this paper has become an important reference in the study of spontaneous imbibition in tight reservoirs, driving rapid development of the “shut-in optimization — capillary-driven oil recovery” engineering direction within China’s unconventional oil and gas sector.

2. Stability analyses of dam abutments by 3D elasto-plastic finite-element method: A case study of Houhe gravity-arch dam in China (2005)#

Description: Using the Houhe gravity-arch dam in China as a case study, this work systematically analyzed dam abutment stability using a 3D elasto-plastic finite element method, providing a numerical simulation framework applicable to real engineering validation. With 39 citations, it was the most impactful work of this phase.

Why it wasn’t done before: 3D elasto-plastic finite element models require significant computational resources, and engineering-grade 3D rock mass simulation was not yet widespread in China before 2005. At the same time, China’s large-scale hydraulic engineering construction (following the Three Gorges Project) provided the case studies and engineering demand that made this work possible.

Impact: It established a methodological template for seismic analysis and dam abutment stability assessment in Chinese gravity-arch dams, and continues to receive sustained citations in the hydraulic and geotechnical engineering literature.

3. Fluid flow with compaction and sand production in unconsolidated sandstone reservoir (2018)#

Description: Through single-phase displacement experiments, this work quantitatively measured the coupled effects of compaction and sand production on fluid flow behavior in unconsolidated sandstones, establishing a fluid flow model that accounts for reservoir damage, and providing experimental evidence for predicting well productivity decline.

Why it wasn’t done before: Specialized high-pressure core holders capable of simultaneously controlling compaction degree and sand production are difficult to fabricate; experimental operations on unconsolidated sandstones (sample preparation and encapsulation) are technically demanding, and relevant experimental data were previously very scarce.

Impact: With 23 citations, it provides an experimental foundation for productivity evaluation and sand control design in heavy oil and loose sandstone reservoirs (such as Liaohe Oilfield).

4. The variation mechanism of petrophysical properties and the effect of compaction on the relative permeability of an unconsolidated sandstone reservoir (2017)#

Description: This work systematically studied the variation of petrophysical parameters (porosity, permeability) with compaction in unconsolidated sandstone reservoirs, particularly quantifying the effect of compaction on gas-water relative permeability curves, and revealing the mechanism behind the decline in flow capacity during production.

Why it wasn’t done before: Relative permeability experiments are typically conducted on consolidated cores; combined dynamic compaction and two-phase flow experiments on unconsolidated sandstones present significant technical and equipment challenges, and such data have long been lacking.

Impact: With 18 citations, it fills a gap in compaction-flow coupling research for unconsolidated sandstones and provides practical reference for development planning in offshore heavy oil and loose sandstone reservoirs.

5. A SCREENING CANDIDATE RESERVOIR FOR GAS INJECTION WAY BASED ON CHARACTERISTIC PARAMETER’S COMPREHENSIVE WEIGHT OPTIMIZATION METHOD (2004)#

Description: This work proposed a reservoir screening method for gas injection candidates based on comprehensive weight optimization of characteristic parameters, providing a quantitative decision-making framework for reservoir selection in CO₂/N₂/natural gas miscible flooding — a systematic methodological contribution to early EOR engineering practice.

Why it wasn’t done before: Early reservoir screening largely relied on empirical judgment based on single parameters, lacking a multi-parameter quantitative evaluation methodology. Meanwhile, China’s large-scale CO₂-EOR pilot tests (early 2000s) provided the accumulated data needed to refine screening criteria.

Impact: With 6 citations, it provides a reusable systematic screening framework for the preliminary assessment of gas injection EOR projects in Chinese oilfields.

Methodological Evolution#

In the early phase (1996–2004), research was primarily based on engineering empirical methods, including fuzzy analytical hierarchy process (FAHP), parameter optimization screening, and semi-empirical predictive models — emphasizing decision-support tools for oilfield engineering practice with limited experimental validation. The middle phase (2005–2011) shifted methodologically toward numerical simulation (3D elasto-plastic finite element) and geophysical field testing (CPTu, seismic waves, borehole geophysics), reflecting a trend toward greater precision in geotechnical engineering. The later phase (2017–2019) completed the transition from macro-engineering to micro-mechanism research: centered on high-pressure core physical experiments (single-phase/two-phase displacement, compaction loading apparatus) combined with NMR pore-scale characterization, supplemented by numerical simulation and theoretical modeling — representing the mainstream paradigm of contemporary reservoir physics research.
