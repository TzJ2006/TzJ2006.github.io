Research Trajectory#

Xiongfei Yu’s academic trajectory shows a rare cross-disciplinary multi-stage development pattern, likely resulting from the combined efforts of multiple researchers with the same name. The first phase (1996—2004) focused on the field of petroleum engineering, specializing in the prediction of gas condensate production, CO₂ injection for enhanced oil recovery (EOR), and the evaluation of gas injection reservoirs. This work had a clear engineering background from Chinese oilfields such as Liaohe and Daqing. The second phase (2005—2011) shifted the research focus to geotechnical and civil engineering, with contributions including finite element analysis of dam stability, flood protection dams in Louisiana, USA, and pile foundation testing in Georgia. These works had North American engineering characteristics and had little connection to petroleum engineering. The third phase (2017—2019) returned to petroleum engineering, but the research level was elevated to rock physics and unconventional reservoir mechanisms, focusing on the compaction of unconsolidated sandstone, the impact of sand production on fluid flow, and spontaneous imbibition in tight sandstone, reflecting the development of shale oil/tight oil in China. The most cited paper (2019, 108 citations) represents the peak of his academic influence.

Breakthrough Works#

1. New insights into spontaneous imbibition in tight oil sandstones with NMR (2019)#

Description: Using nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) technology, this study systematically revealed the microscopic mechanisms and quantitative laws of spontaneous imbibition in tight sandstone reservoirs. This provides an experimental basis for understanding the mechanism of crude oil production during the post-fracturing period of well layering, and directly guides the optimization of fracturing fluid discharge and well layering systems.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Tight sandstone has extremely small pore sizes (nanometer scale), making it impossible to characterize the distribution and migration of fluids in pores in situ and dynamically using traditional mercury injection and core experiments. The maturity of NMR core analysis technology and the widespread use of high-field instruments provide a prerequisite for such research. Additionally, the large-scale commercial development of shale oil/tight oil in China (since 2015) has created urgent engineering needs, which have driven the availability of experimental funds and sample sources.

Impact: This paper was cited 108 times and became an important reference for studying spontaneous imbibition in tight reservoirs. It has contributed to the rapid development of the engineering direction of “well layering optimization—capillary force oil production” in China’s unconventional oil and gas industry.

2. Stability analyses of dam abutments by 3D elasto-plastic finite-element method: A case study of Houhe gravity-arch dam in China (2005)#

Description: Using a three-dimensional elastic-plastic finite element method, this study systematically analyzed the stability of dam shoulders in China’s Houhe gravity arch dam. It provided a numerical simulation framework that can be used for practical engineering verification. This work was cited 39 times and represents the most influential achievement of this phase.

Why it couldn’t be done before: The use of three-dimensional elastic-plastic finite element models requires high computational resources, and three-dimensional rock body simulations were not widely used in China before 2005. Additionally, large-scale water conservancy projects in China (such as the Three Gorges Project) provided case studies and engineering requirements for this research.

Impact: This work provides a methodological framework for analyzing the seismic resistance and dam shoulder stability of gravity dams in China. It has been continuously cited in literature on civil engineering and geotechnical engineering.

3. Fluid flow with compaction and sand production in unconsolidated sandstone reservoir (2018)#

Description: Through single-phase displacement experiments, this study quantitatively measured the combined effects of compaction and sand production on the seepage behavior of unconsolidated sandstone reservoirs. It established a fluid flow model that takes into account reservoir damage, providing an experimental basis for predicting oil well productivity decline.

Why it couldn’t be done before: It was difficult to implement specialized high-pressure core clamping devices to simultaneously control compaction and sand production. The technical difficulty of experimental operations (sample preparation and encapsulation) for unconsolidated sandstone was relatively high, and previous experimental data were scarce.

Impact: This work was cited 23 times and provided an experimental basis for evaluating productivity and sand prevention designs in heavy oil and loose sandstone reservoirs (such as Liaohe Oilfield).

4. The variation mechanism of petrophysical properties and the effect of compaction on the relative permeability of an unconsolidated sandstone reservoir (2017)#

Description: This study systematically investigated the changes in petrophysical parameters (porosity, permeability) of unconsolidated sandstone reservoirs with compaction. It quantified the impact of compaction on the gas-water relative permeability curve, revealing the mechanism of productivity decline during production.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Relative permeability experiments were usually conducted on consolidated cores. The combination of dynamic compaction and two-phase seepage experiments in unconsolidated sandstone was technically challenging, and such data had been scarce for a long time.

Impact: This work was cited 18 times and filled the gap in research on the coupling between compaction and seepage in unconsolidated sandstone. It has practical reference value for the design of development plans for offshore heavy oil and loose sandstone reservoirs.

5. A SCREENING CANDIDATE RESERVOIR FOR GAS INJECTION WAY BASED ON CHARACTERISTIC PARAMETER’S COMPREHENSIVE WEIGHT OPTIMIZATION METHOD (2004)#

Description: This study proposed a method for screening candidate reservoirs for gas injection based on comprehensive weight optimization of characteristic parameters. It provided a quantitative decision-making framework for selecting reservoirs for CO₂/N₂/gas mixed phase flooding. This was a systematic contribution to the early EOR engineering practices.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Early reservoir screening often relied on empirical judgment of single parameters, lacking a systematic approach to multi-parameter optimization. At the same time, large-scale experiments with CO₂-EOR in China (early 2000s) provided data for refining screening criteria.

Impact: This work was cited 6 times and provided a reusable systematical screening approach for evaluating projects involving gas injection to improve oil recovery in Chinese oilfields.

Research Directions#

  • Enhanced oil recovery through gas injection (CO₂/N₂/gas mixed phase flooding, reservoir screening evaluation)
  • Geotechnical and civil engineering (finite element analysis of dam shoulder stability, geophysical exploration, flood protection dams)
  • Sealing mechanism of unconventional reservoirs and development dynamics prediction

Methodological Evolution#

Early (1996—2004) research relied mainly on engineering experience methods, including fuzzy hierarchical analysis method (FAHP), parameter optimization screening, and semi-empirical prediction models. These were primarily used as decision-making tools in oilfield engineering practices, with little experimental verification. Mid-term (2005—2011) methodology shifted towards numerical simulation (three-dimensional elastic-plastic finite element) and geophysical field testing (CPTu, seismic waves, borehole geophysics), reflecting the trend toward precision in the field of geotechnical engineering. Later period (2017—2019) methodology completed the transition from macroscopic engineering to microscopic mechanisms: focusing on high-pressure core physical experiments (single-phase/two-phase displacement, compaction loading devices) combined with NMR pore-scale characterization, supplemented by numerical simulation and theoretical models. This represents the mainstream paradigm of contemporary reservoir physics research.

Domain Impact#
