Breakthrough Works#

1. A low cost seasonal solar soil heat storage system for greenhouse heating: Design and pilot study (2015)#

Description: Described and experimentally tested a low-cost seasonal solar soil heat storage system for winter heating in greenhouses. This system stores solar energy in the soil during summer and extracts it for use in winter, enabling thermal transfer across seasons.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Seasonal soil heat storage has been difficult to implement due to its complexity and high cost. This paper proposed a simplified design and completed field tests, reducing the engineering barriers. There was no low-cost prototype that had been tested and verified before, so the feasibility of this method was questionable.

Impact: It became a highly cited benchmark document in this field (125 citations), providing an economically feasible technical approach for low-carbon heating in agricultural greenhouses. It has been widely referenced in subsequent research on energy storage and agricultural energy systems.

2. An automated optimization method for calibrating building energy simulation models with measured data: Orientation and a case study (2016)#

Description: Proposed an automated optimization method for calibrating building energy consumption models based on measured data, solving the problem of discrepancies between simulation results and real-world energy consumption. This method automates the manual calibration process.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Building energy consumption models have many parameters (material thermal properties, equipment efficiency, energy usage behavior, etc.). Traditional manual calibration relied on engineers’ experience and was extremely time-consuming. Before 2016, automated optimization algorithms were not yet mature in the field of building simulation, and computational costs limited large-scale parameter searches.

Impact: It received 87 citations, becoming an important reference for methodology in calibrating building energy consumption models. It promoted the use of automation in digital twin and Building Information Modeling (BIM) fields.

3. Global sensitivity analysis of an urban microclimate system under uncertainty: Design and case study (2017)#

Description: Systematically introduced global sensitivity analysis into urban microclimate simulation, quantifying the contribution of various input parameters to the uncertainty in predicting urban thermal environments and identifying key influencing factors.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Urban microclimate model parameters are extremely complex (building geometry, materials, vegetation, meteorology, etc.). Local sensitivity analysis cannot capture the interaction effects between parameters. Before 2017, calculating the global sensitivity analysis using Sobol’s method was very computationally expensive. It became feasible only after high-performance computing became more common.

Impact: It received 76 citations, providing a methodological framework for quantifying uncertainties in urban climate models. It promoted the evaluation of model credibility in climate-adaptive urban planning.

4. The airborne transmission of infection between flats in high-rise residential buildings: A review (2015)#

Description: Systematically reviewed the mechanisms of droplet and aerosol transmission between floors in high-rise residential buildings, focusing on the paths of cross-infection driven by单侧自然通风. Public health risks were also evaluated.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Cross-floor aerosol transmission attracted academic attention only after SARS (2003). However, relevant experimental and simulation data was not accumulated until 2015 to support a systematic review. High-rise dense residential patterns are unique to East Asia, so Western literature covered this area insufficiently.

Impact: It received 61 citations. It was widely referenced during the COVID-19 pandemic and became an important reference for evaluating infection risks in high-rise buildings. It influenced discussions on ventilation design specifications.

5. Planning Low-carbon Distributed Power Systems: Evaluating the Role of Energy Storage (2020)#

Description: Incorporated energy storage systems (dynamic charge-discharge efficiency models) into the planning framework for expanding power generation capacity, evaluating the economic and technical value of energy storage in the decarbonization path of distributed power systems.

Why it couldn’t be done before: Early capacity planning models simplified energy storage as static devices, ignoring the dynamic characteristics of charge-discharge efficiency with state changes. Before 2020, the cost of lithium batteries had not significantly decreased, so the commercial significance of incorporating energy storage into the planning model was limited. With the rapid decline in energy storage costs, the need for accurate modeling became urgent.

Impact: It received 46 citations, providing a tool for power system planners to quantify the value of energy storage. It has certain application value in decision-making support for renewable energy absorption and grid decarbonization.
