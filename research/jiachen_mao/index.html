Breakthrough Works#

1. A low cost seasonal solar soil heat storage system for greenhouse heating: Design and pilot study (2015)#

Description: Designed and experimentally validated a low-cost seasonal solar soil heat storage system for winter greenhouse heating, achieving cross-seasonal thermal energy transfer by storing solar heat in the soil during summer and extracting it in winter.

Why it wasn’t done before: Seasonal soil heat storage had long been impractical due to construction complexity and high costs. This paper proposed a simplified design and completed field validation, lowering the engineering barrier. Prior to this, there was no empirically validated low-cost prototype, and engineering feasibility remained in question.

Impact: Became a highly cited benchmark in this niche area (125 citations), providing an economically viable technical pathway for low-carbon heating in agricultural greenhouses, and has been widely cited in subsequent energy storage and agricultural energy research.

2. An automated optimization method for calibrating building energy simulation models with measured data: Orientation and a case study (2016)#

Description: Proposed an automated optimization method for calibrating building energy simulation models against measured data, addressing the discrepancy between simulation results and actual energy consumption by automating the manual calibration process.

Why it wasn’t done before: Building energy models have numerous parameters (thermal properties of materials, equipment efficiency, occupant behavior, etc.), and traditional manual calibration relied heavily on engineer expertise and was extremely time-consuming. Before 2016, automated optimization algorithms were not yet mature in the building simulation domain, and computational costs further constrained large-scale parameter search.

Impact: With 87 citations, it has become an important methodological reference for building energy model calibration, advancing automated calibration practices in digital twin and Building Information Modeling (BIM) domains.

3. Global sensitivity analysis of an urban microclimate system under uncertainty: Design and case study (2017)#

Description: Systematically introduced Global Sensitivity Analysis (GSA) into urban microclimate simulation, quantifying the uncertainty contribution of each input parameter to urban thermal environment predictions and identifying the most influential factors.

Why it wasn’t done before: Urban microclimate models have extremely high parameter dimensionality (building geometry, materials, vegetation, meteorology, etc.), and local sensitivity analysis cannot capture interaction effects between parameters. Before 2017, the computational overhead of coupling Sobol and other global GSA methods with urban microclimate solvers was prohibitive; it only became feasible after high-performance computing became more accessible.

Impact: Cited 76 times, it established a methodological framework for uncertainty quantification in urban climate models and advanced model credibility assessment in climate-adaptive urban planning.

4. The airborne transmission of infection between flats in high-rise residential buildings: A review (2015)#

Description: Systematically reviewed the mechanisms of droplet and aerosol transmission between units in high-rise residential buildings, with a focus on cross-floor infection pathways driven by single-sided natural ventilation, and assessed the associated public health risks.

Why it wasn’t done before: Cross-floor aerosol transmission only began attracting scholarly attention after SARS (2003), but sufficient experimental and simulation data did not accumulate to support a systematic review until 2015. High-density high-rise living is a context specific to East Asia, and Western literature had previously provided inadequate coverage.

Impact: Cited 61 times, it was widely referenced during the COVID-19 pandemic and became an important reference for infection risk assessment in high-rise buildings, influencing discussions on ventilation design standards.

5. Planning Low-carbon Distributed Power Systems: Evaluating the Role of Energy Storage (2020)#

Description: Incorporated energy storage systems (with a dynamic charge/discharge efficiency model) into a generation capacity expansion planning framework, evaluating the techno-economic value of storage in decarbonization pathways for distributed power systems.

Why it wasn’t done before: Early capacity planning models treated energy storage as a static device, ignoring the dynamic nature of charge/discharge efficiency as a function of state. Before 2020, lithium battery costs had not yet fallen dramatically, limiting the commercial relevance of including storage in planning models; accurate modeling only became urgent as storage costs dropped rapidly.

Impact: Cited 46 times, it provides power system planners with a quantitative tool for evaluating storage value, with practical applications in renewable energy integration and grid decarbonization decision support.
